Understanding Melasma: Causes, Triggers, and Treatments

By Dr. Punit Sakhare, MD Dermatologist · 2026-07-06 · 6 min read

Melasma is one of the most common skin concerns I see — brown or grey-brown patches on the cheeks, forehead, upper lip or jawline. It's also one of the most frustrating, because it's chronic. Here's what every patient with melasma should understand.

What Melasma Actually Is

Melasma is a chronic, acquired form of hypermelanosis — symmetric, irregularly bordered brown-to-grey-brown patches, typically on the cheeks, forehead, upper lip or jawline (Handel et al., 2014). It affects women far more than men, especially in the 20–40 age range. Indian skin (Fitzpatrick IV–V) is particularly prone to it.

The Real Causes

Melasma is triggered by hormones (pregnancy — hence the name "mask of pregnancy" — birth control, hormonal IUDs), UV light, heat, blue light from screens, and inflammation from skincare products. Pregnancy is one of the most consistent hormonal triggers: a global survey of 324 women with melasma found 26% reported onset during pregnancy and 42% reported onset in the months after delivery (Ortonne et al., 2009). Genetics play a role too — over 40% of patients in published case series report an affected first-degree relative (Handel et al., 2014).

Why It's So Stubborn

Melasma isn't on the skin surface. The pigment sits in deeper layers, which is why creams alone rarely fix it. And because hormones and sunlight keep triggering new pigment, treatment doesn't "cure" melasma — it manages it.

Step 1: Aggressive Sun Protection

This is non-negotiable. Broad-spectrum SPF 50+ every single day, reapplied every 3 hours. Also blue-light-blocking sunscreens for the hours you're on screens. Without this, no other treatment will work.

Step 2: Topical Brightening

Prescription-strength topicals: hydroquinone — the most-studied and most effective single pigment-lightening agent for melasma in clinical trial reviews (Gupta et al., 2006) — along with tretinoin, kojic acid, azelaic acid, and niacinamide. These reduce pigment production and speed cell turnover. Compounded formulas often work better than off-the-shelf products for Indian skin. Anyone who is pregnant or breastfeeding should discuss timing with a dermatologist first — some of these actives, including oral and topical retinoids, carry separate pregnancy-specific precautions covered in our melasma-in-pregnancy guide (Chien et al., 2016).

Step 3: In-Clinic Treatments

For deeper melasma, we combine topical care with in-clinic chemical peels (glycolic, TCA), Q-Switched Nd:YAG laser at low fluences (never high — high settings can worsen melasma), and occasionally microneedling.

What NOT to Do

Avoid: aggressive lasers on Indian skin (they can trigger paradoxical darkening), IPL for melasma (can worsen it), excessive scrubbing, harsh acids without dermatologist guidance, and "fairness" creams with unknown ingredients.

Managing Long-Term

Melasma is chronic. Expect ongoing maintenance — even after clearance, you'll need to continue sun protection and periodic top-up treatments to prevent recurrence. This is normal and manageable.

The Emotional Piece

Melasma can affect confidence. Progress is slow (3–6 months for visible change). Be patient, stay consistent, and work with a dermatologist who understands Indian skin. Rushing treatment usually makes melasma worse.
