Dark Circles

What Deficiency Causes Dark Circles? Separating Nutrition Myths from Dermatology Evidence

By Dr. Punit Sakhare, MD Dermatologist · 2026-07-21 · 8 min read

Quick Facts

The short answer
For most people, no single vitamin or mineral deficiency causes dark circles. Periorbital darkening is usually genetic, pigmentary, vascular, or structural — iron-deficiency anemia is the one nutritional link with real clinical evidence.
What's actually evidence-backed
Iron-deficiency anemia can cause periorbital pallor (a washed-out, pale appearance) because thin under-eye skin reveals reduced blood hemoglobin more readily than skin elsewhere.
What histology shows
Biopsies of darkened periorbital skin show increased melanin and dilated blood vessels — not deposited iron (hemosiderin), which was absent in every case in one published study.
Popular myths with little evidence
Vitamin K, vitamin C, vitamin E, "toxin buildup," and dehydration are widely cited online but are not part of the evidence-based causation or treatment literature for dark circles.
Strongest actual risk factor
Genetics and family history — periorbital hyperpigmentation reviews list heredity as a primary, well-recognized cause, alongside allergy-related skin inflammation and age-related volume loss.
When to see a doctor instead of taking a supplement
If pallor under the eyes comes with fatigue, breathlessness, or paleness of the palms and nail beds, ask for a hemoglobin/ferritin blood test rather than self-treating with vitamins.

Search "what deficiency causes dark circles" and you'll find dozens of confident answers pointing to vitamin K, vitamin C, iron, or some combination of all three. The honest, evidence-based answer is more specific: most dark circles aren't caused by a nutritional deficiency at all, and the one nutrient that does have a real, documented connection — iron, via anemia — works through a different mechanism than most people assume. This article separates what dermatology and hematology research actually supports from what's simply repeated often enough online to sound established.

Why "Deficiency" Is the Wrong First Question

Framing dark circles as a deficiency problem assumes a single missing nutrient is the root cause, the same way people often ask what vitamin they're "missing" for hair loss or brittle nails. Published dermatology reviews describe periorbital hyperpigmentation (the clinical term for dark, discolored skin around the eyes) very differently: as a multifactorial condition, meaning several overlapping mechanisms usually combine in the same person to produce the appearance, rather than one single cause explaining most cases.

A comprehensive review of the condition's causes lists genetic and hereditary factors, excess pigment production, post-inflammatory pigmentation from skin conditions like allergic or atopic dermatitis, periorbital swelling, visibly dilated blood vessels, and shadowing from skin laxity or volume loss with aging (Sarkar et al., 2016). A separate systematic review of 39 treatment studies groups the underlying causes into four categories: pigmentary (excess melanin), vascular (visible or dilated blood vessels showing through thin skin), structural (shadowing from lost fat volume, bony orbital shape, or skin laxity), and mixed presentations combining more than one of the above (Michelle et al., 2021).

Nutritional deficiency isn't a standalone category in either framework. That doesn't mean nutrition is entirely irrelevant — it means the evidence doesn't support treating "which vitamin am I missing" as the default first question, which is the finding the rest of this article works through in detail, category by category.

The One Nutritional Link With Real Evidence: Iron-Deficiency Anemia

Iron-deficiency anemia is the exception worth taking seriously, and the mechanism is straightforward physiology rather than a marketing claim. Iron is a required building block for hemoglobin, the oxygen-carrying, red-pigmented protein inside red blood cells; when the body doesn't have enough iron, it makes less hemoglobin, and blood carries visibly less red pigment. Skin around the eyes is among the thinnest on the body — roughly 0.5 mm at the eyelid versus closer to 2 mm on the cheek — so it shows underlying blood volume and tone changes more readily than thicker skin does anywhere else on the face.

A systematic review of physical examination techniques for diagnosing anemia found that periorbital and conjunctival (the membrane lining the inner eyelid) pallor is a real, recognized clinical sign, though an imperfect one on its own: sensitivity ranged from 19–97% and specificity from 65–100% across the fourteen studies reviewed, and combining pallor checks across nine or more body sites — palms, nail beds, conjunctiva, and periorbital skin together — raised both sensitivity and specificity substantially compared with relying on any single spot (Vyas et al., 2022).

In practice, that means periorbital pallor can reflect anemia, but it's too variable on its own to self-diagnose from — and importantly, it looks like a washed-out, pale or grayish tone rather than the brown or purplish pigmentation most people picture when they say "dark circles." Confirming or ruling out anemia requires a blood test measuring hemoglobin and ferritin (the body's iron-storage protein), not an eye cream or a guess based on appearance alone.

What the Skin Actually Looks Like Under a Microscope

One study took biopsies from darkened periorbital skin and matched, non-darkened control skin from behind the ear in 28 patients with the condition formally known as cutaneous idiopathic hyperchromia of the orbital region (CIHOR) — essentially, unexplained dark circles with no other identified cause. The most consistent finding was an increase in melanin (the skin's natural brown-black pigment, produced by cells called melanocytes) in the upper dermis, more pronounced in more visually severe cases, along with dilated blood vessels in the deeper dermis. Hemosiderin — the iron-containing pigment that deposits in tissue when blood breaks down locally, as it does in a healing bruise — was absent in every single biopsy across all severity levels (Graziosi et al., 2013).

That finding matters because it directly separates two ideas people often conflate. Having too little iron circulating in the blood (anemia, which can cause pallor, as covered above) is not the same phenomenon as iron somehow accumulating locally under the eyes to cause darkening — a claim sometimes made about "pooled blood" or iron pigment under thin skin. The tissue itself shows increased melanin and dilated vessels, not deposited iron. So even in people who do have anemia, the classic brown or purplish pigmented dark-circle appearance isn't explained by iron sitting in the skin — it's a separate mechanism entirely, which is exactly why correcting an iron deficiency doesn't reliably erase pigmented dark circles that were already present.

The Nutrition Myths That Don't Hold Up

Vitamin K, vitamin C, vitamin E, collagen supplements, "toxin flushing," and dehydration are the claims that circulate most widely as dark-circle fixes. None of them appear in the evidence-based causation frameworks above, and none appear among the treatments with actual clinical trial support. The systematic review of 39 published treatment studies for periorbital hyperpigmentation identifies topical depigmenting agents (hydroquinone, kojic acid, azelaic acid, retinoic acid), chemical peels, lasers, dermal fillers, autologous fat grafting, carboxytherapy, platelet-rich plasma injections, blepharoplasty surgery, and normobaric oxygen as the interventions with published evidence — matched to whether the cause is pigment, vessels, or lost volume (Michelle et al., 2021; Sarkar et al., 2016). Correcting a specific vitamin intake is notably absent from that list.

Vitamin K is worth singling out because it has a plausible-sounding mechanism: it's involved in clotting-factor production, and some eye creams have historically included it on the theory that it might reduce bruise-like discoloration from tiny leaking capillaries. But a mechanism that sounds plausible isn't the same as clinical proof, and it hasn't translated into the kind of controlled trial evidence that would place it alongside hydroquinone or laser therapy in a systematic review. Vitamin C and E are typically included in dark-circle products as general antioxidants rather than because a documented deficiency of either has been linked to periorbital pigmentation. "Toxin buildup" isn't a defined medical mechanism at all — it doesn't correspond to any pathway described in the pigmentary, vascular, or structural causation model above.

None of this means general nutrition is irrelevant to skin health broadly. It means the specific claim "take vitamin X and your dark circles will fade" isn't backed by the same quality of evidence as the anemia-pallor connection or the histology findings above. Be skeptical of any product or advice that names a single vitamin as "the" cause of dark circles without citing a mechanism or a study.

When Dark Circles Are Worth Mentioning to a Doctor

Most dark circles are cosmetic, not medical. But a few patterns are worth a conversation with a physician rather than a trip to the supplement aisle:

  • Periorbital pallor plus systemic symptoms — fatigue, breathlessness, or noticeable paleness of the palms and nail beds alongside the under-eye change — warrants a hemoglobin and ferritin blood test to check for anemia, per the physical-exam accuracy data above (Vyas et al., 2022).
  • Darkening that tracks with allergies or eye rubbing — chronic allergic or atopic skin inflammation around the eyes is a recognized, separate cause of post-inflammatory pigmentation, distinct from anemia or genetics (Sarkar et al., 2016).
  • Sudden or one-sided changes — asymmetric or rapidly changing pigmentation, swelling, or discoloration should always be evaluated by a dermatologist rather than assumed to be routine dark circles.

Outside of these patterns, family history remains the single most consistently cited factor in the literature — if your parents have prominent under-eye darkening, genetics is a more likely explanation than diet.

What Actually Helps, Once the Cause Is Clear

Because periorbital hyperpigmentation has genuinely different underlying causes, the evidence-based treatments are matched to which one applies: topical agents and chemical peels for pigment-driven darkening, vascular-targeted lasers for visible-vessel cases, and fillers, fat grafting, or blepharoplasty for volume loss and shadowing from aging (Michelle et al., 2021). If a blood test does confirm iron-deficiency anemia, treating it under a physician's guidance addresses the systemic cause of pallor — though it won't necessarily reverse pre-existing melanin or vascular changes in the skin, since those persist somewhat independently of blood counts (Graziosi et al., 2013).

The Bottom Line

"What deficiency causes dark circles" has a more precise answer than most search results suggest: for the majority of people, the cause is genetic, pigmentary, vascular, or structural, not nutritional. Iron-deficiency anemia is the one nutrition-adjacent factor with real supporting evidence, and even then, it produces pallor rather than the pigmented darkening most people are describing. Popular claims about vitamin K, vitamin C, or "toxins" simply aren't part of the peer-reviewed causation or treatment literature. If under-eye darkening bothers you, the more useful next step is identifying which of the actual documented mechanisms applies to your case — ideally with a dermatologist — rather than reaching for a supplement first.
