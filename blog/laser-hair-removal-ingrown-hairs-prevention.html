Ingrown Hairs After Laser Hair Removal: Why They Happen and How to Prevent Them

By Dr. Punit Sakhare, MD Dermatologist · 2026-07-18 · 8 min read

Quick Facts

What's actually happening
Treated hairs shed and work their way out through the skin over 1-3 weeks. That process can trigger a short-lived follicular inflammation mechanistically similar to pseudofolliculitis barbae.
How long it lasts
Laser-induced folliculitis is described in the literature as a self-limited complication that typically settles on its own.
Who's more prone
Curly or coarse hair increases risk, linked in research to a genetic variant in a hair-specific keratin gene.
The bigger picture
Laser hair removal isn't just a possible short-term irritant — it's an established long-term treatment for chronic ingrown hairs and pseudofolliculitis barbae once a course is complete.
What helps
Gentle cleansing rather than harsh scrubbing while a follicle is actively shedding reduces the mechanical irritation that drives these bumps.

If you've noticed a few small bumps or what looks like an ingrown hair in the days after a laser hair removal session, it's a fair question to ask whether the treatment caused the very problem it's supposed to solve. The honest, evidence-based answer is more reassuring than it sounds: a short-lived bump or two while a treated hair sheds is a recognised, self-limited reaction — and it's mechanistically different from the ingrown hairs that shaving and waxing cause over and over again. In fact, once a full course is complete, laser hair removal is used by dermatologists specifically to treat chronic ingrown-hair conditions.

What's Actually Happening Under the Skin

Laser hair removal disables the hair follicle, but it doesn't instantly remove the hair shaft that's already there. Over the following one to three weeks, that treated hair sheds and works its way out through the skin's surface — a process your OPAL Radiant care instructions describe as normal, not a sign of regrowth. According to a case report and mechanism review published in the Journal of Clinical and Aesthetic Dermatology, the friction and disruption of hairs pushing through the skin during this shedding phase can act as a "nidus," or trigger point, for a localised inflammatory response — clinically similar to the mechanism behind pseudofolliculitis barbae, the classic shaving-related ingrown hair condition (Schuler et al., 2020). The authors describe this laser-induced folliculitis as a self-limited complication, meaning it resolves on its own without lasting effect, though it can be uncomfortable enough in the moment to worry patients unfamiliar with the process (Schuler et al., 2020).

Why This Is Different From the Ingrown Hairs You Get From Shaving or Waxing

Classic pseudofolliculitis barbae — the recurring ingrown-hair cycle familiar to anyone who shaves or waxes regularly — happens because repeatedly cutting or pulling hair at or below the skin surface encourages the regrowing hair to curl back and re-enter the skin, triggering a chronic, foreign-body-type inflammatory reaction each time (Ribera et al., 2010). A review in the British Journal of Dermatology describes this as a genuinely "vexing" cycle: the hair itself is the irritant, and as long as hair keeps regrowing and getting cut or pulled the same way, the cycle repeats indefinitely (Jasterzbski & Schwartz, 2015).

Laser hair removal interrupts that cycle rather than feeding it. The bumps that occasionally appear right after a session are tied to hairs shedding out for the last time as the follicle is disabled — not a repeating pattern of new ingrown hairs being created session after session. That's the key mechanistic difference between "a treated hair working its way out once" and "the same follicle re-irritating itself every time you shave."

Who's More Likely to Notice This

Not everyone reacts the same way. The research on pseudofolliculitis consistently points to hair type as the biggest risk factor: curly and coarse hair is more prone to curving back into the follicle or surrounding skin than straight hair. A British Journal of Dermatology review notes that curly hair, combined with a specific single-nucleotide variant in the gene for a hair-specific keratin (keratin 75), appears to act together to raise the risk of this kind of follicular inflammation (Jasterzbski & Schwartz, 2015). This is also why pseudofolliculitis barbae has historically been studied and reported most often in people with tightly curled hair (Ribera et al., 2010; Dalia et al., 2023) — the same population that tends to see the bumps described above after a laser session, even though the underlying mechanism (shedding, not regrowth) is different.

The Bigger Picture: Laser Is a Treatment for Ingrown Hairs, Not Just a Possible Cause

This is the point most people don't expect: for patients who already struggle with chronic ingrown hairs or pseudofolliculitis barbae from shaving or waxing, laser hair removal is one of the evidence-based treatment options dermatologists reach for. A 2023 treatment review in Clinical and Experimental Dermatology lists laser hair removal alongside topical therapies, keratolytics and antibiotics as a recommended option, noting it "has become a potential long-term treatment option" for pseudofolliculitis barbae, with additional research ongoing into its long-term durability (Dalia et al., 2023). The British Journal of Dermatology review is similarly direct: laser hair removal is described as "safe and effective in most skin types" for treating this condition, with the important caveat that the laser system and settings must be properly matched to the patient's skin type — exactly the kind of skin-type-specific calibration OPAL Radiant's clinicians already do at every consultation (Jasterzbski & Schwartz, 2015).

Put simply: a temporary bump while hair sheds after a session is a short-term nuisance on the way to a longer-term reduction in exactly this problem — not evidence that laser is making ingrown hairs worse over time.

Evidence-Based Prevention and Aftercare

Since the trigger for laser-induced folliculitis is mechanical disruption of a follicle that's actively shedding a hair, the practical prevention advice follows directly from the mechanism itself:

  • Cleanse gently, don't scrub. The proposed management approach for laser-induced folliculitis specifically calls out "gentle washing techniques" as part of reducing severity and duration (Schuler et al., 2020) — the same logic behind avoiding harsh exfoliation while a follicle is inflamed.
  • Resist the urge to pick or squeeze. Traditional pseudofolliculitis management likewise emphasises avoiding further mechanical trauma to the area as a cornerstone of prevention (Ribera et al., 2010).
  • Hold off on harsher exfoliants for the first few days. There isn't a single published trial that pins an exact "safe day" to resume scrubs or exfoliating acids specifically after laser hair removal — this is standard clinical practice guidance rather than a dated research finding, and it's the approach OPAL Radiant follows: let any initial redness and follicle activity settle for the first few days before reintroducing anything abrasive on the treated area.
  • If bumps persist or worsen rather than settling on their own, it's worth a call to your clinic. In more persistent or recurrent cases, the literature notes dermatologists may consider short-course prophylactic measures such as doxycycline or topical steroids for future sessions in patients known to react this way (Schuler et al., 2020) — but this is a clinical decision for your dermatologist to make, not something to self-treat.

The Bottom Line

A bump or two while a treated hair sheds is a recognised, self-limited part of the laser hair removal process for some people — mechanistically distinct from the repeating ingrown-hair cycle that shaving and waxing cause, and, for people who already deal with chronic ingrown hairs, laser hair removal is actually one of the evidence-based long-term treatments available (Dalia et al., 2023; Jasterzbski & Schwartz, 2015). Gentle handling of the area in the days after your session — cleansing without scrubbing, and holding off on harsh exfoliants until any redness has settled — is the most evidence-aligned way to minimise irritation while your skin does the rest of the work on its own.
