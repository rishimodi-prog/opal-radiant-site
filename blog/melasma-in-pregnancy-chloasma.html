Melasma in Pregnancy: What Causes the "Mask of Pregnancy" and What the Evidence Says About Treating It

By Dr. Punit Sakhare, MD Dermatologist · 2026-07-17 · 9 min read

Quick Facts

What it's called
Melasma that appears or worsens during pregnancy is often called chloasma gravidarum or the "mask of pregnancy" — it's the same underlying condition as melasma from other hormonal triggers, not a separate disease.
Main driver
Rising estrogen, progesterone, and melanocyte-stimulating hormone (MSH) during pregnancy stimulate melanocytes to overproduce pigment in sun-exposed skin.
How common
In a global survey of 324 women with melasma, 26% reported onset during pregnancy and 42% reported onset in the months after delivery, often years later.
Does it fade on its own
Many cases lighten as hormone levels normalize postpartum, but a meaningful proportion persists and needs active treatment.
Treatments generally avoided while pregnant
Oral retinoids are absolutely contraindicated; hydroquinone and high-strength topical retinoids are typically avoided as a precaution due to limited pregnancy safety data.
Safer first-line approach
Strict broad-spectrum sunscreen, physical sun avoidance, and (in some cases) topical azelaic acid — with hydroquinone-based or peel-based regimens usually reserved for after delivery and breastfeeding.

Melasma that shows up during pregnancy has its own name — chloasma gravidarum, or the "mask of pregnancy" — but it isn't a separate skin condition. It's the same disorder covered in our general guide to melasma, triggered here by one specific and very common cause: pregnancy hormones. This article focuses on that hormonal mechanism specifically — why pregnancy is such a strong trigger, how often it happens, and — because this is the question we get asked most in consultation — which treatments are actually considered safe to use while pregnant or breastfeeding, and which ones dermatology guidance says to avoid.

What Melasma Actually Is

Melasma is a chronic, acquired form of hypermelanosis — a fancy way of saying the skin produces and deposits more pigment (melanin) than normal in specific areas. It typically appears as symmetric, irregularly bordered brown-to-grey-brown patches on sun-exposed parts of the face: the cheeks, forehead, upper lip, and jawline (Handel et al., 2014).

It's overwhelmingly a condition of women of reproductive age, and skin biopsy studies cited in the dermatology literature show two things happening at once: the melanocytes (pigment-producing cells) in affected skin are more numerous, and each one is working harder, producing more melanin than melanocytes in unaffected skin nearby (Handel et al., 2014). Genetics play a real role too — over 40% of patients in published case series report an affected first-degree relative — but genetics alone doesn't explain why melasma so often appears, or gets dramatically worse, during pregnancy specifically.

Doctors sometimes describe three patterns based on where pigment sits in the skin: epidermal (in the top layer, usually the most responsive to topical treatment), dermal (deeper, harder to treat), and mixed. Which pattern a person has isn't something you can tell just by looking in the mirror — it typically requires a dermatologist's assessment under specific lighting (a Wood's lamp) — and it meaningfully affects how quickly any treatment, pregnancy-safe or not, is likely to work.

The Hormonal Mechanism: Estrogen, Progesterone, and MSH

Pregnancy produces one of the largest, fastest hormonal shifts the body goes through outside of puberty. Estrogen and progesterone levels rise substantially across the three trimesters, and melanocytes carry receptors for both hormones — meaning they respond directly to this hormonal surge, not just to sun exposure. Alongside this, pregnancy is associated with elevated melanocyte-stimulating hormone (MSH), a pituitary hormone whose entire job is to instruct melanocytes to produce more melanin (Handel et al., 2014).

The result is a skin environment primed for pigment overproduction, layered on top of ultraviolet (UV) exposure, which is the other major trigger. Neither factor alone is usually sufficient — melasma tends to appear where hormonal sensitivity and UV exposure overlap, which is exactly why it concentrates on the parts of the face that get the most sun.

This same hormonal pathway explains why melasma isn't unique to pregnancy — combined oral contraceptives and hormone replacement therapy are recognized triggers for the same reason, since they raise circulating estrogen and progesterone in a comparable way (Handel et al., 2014). Pregnancy simply produces a larger, faster hormonal swing than most other everyday triggers, which is part of why chloasma gravidarum can appear or worsen quickly, sometimes within a single trimester.

Chloasma Gravidarum: How Common Is It, and Who Gets It?

A global survey conducted by the Pigmentary Disorders Academy, an international group of pigmentation specialists, questioned 324 women being treated for melasma across nine clinics worldwide. Pregnancy and hormonal contraception were confirmed as two of the most consistent triggers reported (Ortonne et al., 2009).

Some specific numbers from that survey are worth knowing. Melasma most commonly first appeared after pregnancy — reported by 42% of women, sometimes years after delivery — while 26% reported onset during pregnancy itself, and 29% reported it began even before their first pregnancy. Risk of new-onset melasma during pregnancy rose with more time spent outdoors (roughly a 27% higher odds for every extra 10 hours a week spent outside) and with increasing maternal age at pregnancy. Women who had multiple pregnancies had substantially higher odds than those with one — roughly double the odds with a second pregnancy and triple with a third or more, compared to a single pregnancy (Ortonne et al., 2009). Darker skin phototypes (Fitzpatrick III–VI) and a family history of melasma also raised the likelihood of it appearing postpartum.

What Dermatology Guidance Says to Avoid While Pregnant

This is the part most expecting or breastfeeding mothers actually want answered, and it deserves precision rather than blanket reassurance or blanket fear.

Oral retinoids (like isotretinoin, sometimes used for severe acne) are unambiguous: they are teratogenic and absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy or if pregnancy is being considered (Chien et al., 2016). This isn't specific to melasma treatment, but it's the clearest line in the entire topical-treatment landscape.

Topical retinoids (tretinoin, adapalene, retinol) are a greyer area. Systemic absorption through skin is minimal, and topical tretinoin is not classified as a proven human developmental toxicant the way oral retinoids are — but because large, controlled human safety trials specifically in pregnant women are lacking, most dermatology guidance still recommends avoiding topical retinoids during pregnancy as a precaution (Chien et al., 2016).

Hydroquinone — the most-studied and most effective single pigment-lightening agent for melasma in the general population (Gupta et al., 2006) — has a similar problem: systemic absorption is higher than with retinoids, and pregnancy-specific safety data is limited, so it's routinely avoided during pregnancy and breastfeeding out of caution rather than confirmed harm.

What's Considered Safer to Use

Given the trigger is hormonal-plus-UV, the single highest-impact, lowest-risk intervention is also the most boring one: daily, diligent, broad-spectrum sunscreen, reapplied through the day, plus physical sun avoidance (hats, seeking shade) during peak UV hours. Since UV exposure measurably increases the odds of melasma onset and worsening during pregnancy (Ortonne et al., 2009), this single habit addresses one of the two drivers directly — and it carries no pregnancy safety concerns at all.

Azelaic acid is generally considered one of the better-tolerated topical options in pregnancy; it's a recommended baseline therapy for pregnancy-related acne for the same reason (Chien et al., 2016) and appears in melasma treatment reviews as an effective lightening agent with a favorable safety profile compared with hydroquinone (Gupta et al., 2006). Many clinicians extend that same reasoning to melasma, though this should always be confirmed with your obstetrician and dermatologist given individual variation.

Camouflage makeup, gentle exfoliation, and vitamin C serums are lower-evidence but low-risk supportive options many patients use in the meantime. In-clinic procedures like chemical peels exist as effective adjuncts to lightening regimens generally (Gupta et al., 2006), but given the lack of controlled pregnancy safety data for peeling agents, these are usually deferred until after delivery — and after breastfeeding is cleared with your doctor, if applicable.

After Delivery: Does It Fade, and When Can Treatment Start?

For many women, melasma that appeared during pregnancy lightens meaningfully in the months after delivery as estrogen, progesterone, and MSH return toward baseline. But "many" isn't "all" — melasma has a well-documented tendency to be chronic and relapsing even outside of pregnancy (Handel et al., 2014), and a real proportion of postpartum cases persist and need active treatment to improve, rather than resolving on their own. This is consistent with survey data showing that melasma most commonly first appears after delivery rather than during pregnancy itself (Ortonne et al., 2009) — for some women, the hormonal drop after birth, combined with sun exposure in the following months, is what actually triggers visible pigment, not the pregnancy hormones at their peak.

Hormonal shifts after pregnancy don't only affect pigment — the same estrogen and progesterone swings that trigger melasma can also temporarily affect hair growth cycles, which is why some women notice changes in how their skin and hair respond to laser hair removal in the months after giving birth. Once you're cleared postpartum (and, if relevant, once breastfeeding has ended or your doctor has approved specific ingredients), the full treatment toolkit — hydroquinone-based combination creams, topical retinoids, chemical peels, and in some cases laser or light-based treatments — becomes available again, generally with faster and more reliable results than trying to manage pigment during pregnancy with a narrower, safer ingredient list. There's no fixed timeline that applies to everyone; it depends on breastfeeding status, how the melasma has behaved so far, and what a dermatologist finds on examination.

When to See a Dermatologist

A dermatologist visit is worth it even before you start any treatment, for a simple reason: several other pigmentary conditions can look similar to melasma on casual inspection, and confirming the diagnosis changes what treatment actually makes sense. Dr. Punit Sakhare notes that in clinical practice, patients often self-diagnose melasma when the pattern, distribution, or history points to something else — so a proper visual assessment before committing to any regimen, pregnant or not, avoids months of using the wrong products.

The practical bottom line: chloasma gravidarum is a real, common, and well-understood hormonal response to pregnancy — not a sign that something has gone wrong. Sun protection is safe and effective at any stage. Anything beyond that, especially hydroquinone or retinoid-based products, should be discussed with both your obstetrician and a dermatologist before use, and more aggressive treatment is usually best planned for after delivery.
