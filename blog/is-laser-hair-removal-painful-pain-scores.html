Is Laser Hair Removal Painful? Pain Scores from Clinical Studies, by Body Area

By Dr. Punit Sakhare, MD Dermatologist · 2026-08-09 · 8 min read

Quick Facts

Average pain rating
Most patients score laser hair removal 3–6 out of 10 on a Visual Analog Scale (VAS) — commonly described as a warm rubber-band snap, not a sharp or lasting pain.
Least sensitive areas
Legs, arms, and back — thicker skin and lower nerve-ending density mean lower reported pain in clinical use.
Most sensitive areas
Upper lip, bikini line/Brazilian, and underarms — thin skin, dense nerve supply, and proximity to bone or mucosa.
What reduces pain most, per trials
Topical lidocaine-tetracaine anesthetic and lower, fixed energy (fluence) settings outperformed cooling used alone.
Fluence effect on pain
A randomized trial recorded significantly lower VAS pain with low, fixed-fluence settings (4.26/10) than with higher incremental settings (5.64/10).
Pain trend across sessions
Pain scores generally decline with each subsequent session as treated follicles thin and absorb less laser energy.

"How much does it hurt?" is usually the second question after "does it work?" — and it deserves the same evidence-based answer. Pain during laser hair removal is real, measurable, and has been quantified in published trials using the same 0–10 Visual Analog Scale (VAS) doctors use for other procedures. It is not uniform: the wavelength, the fluence (energy density) setting, the cooling method, and the body area all change the number. This article walks through what those numbers actually are, why some areas hurt more than others, and which pain-reduction techniques hold up in clinical comparisons versus which are mostly marketing.

What the Pain Actually Feels Like — and Why It Happens

Laser hair removal works through selective photothermolysis: the laser wavelength is absorbed by melanin (pigment) concentrated in the hair follicle and converted to heat, which disables the follicle. That heat is what you feel — it activates heat-sensitive nerve endings (nociceptors) in the upper dermis for a fraction of a second per pulse. Most patients describe it as a warm pinch or a rubber band snapping against the skin, not a cutting or burning pain that lingers.

Because the sensation is thermal and tied directly to how much energy the follicle absorbs, anything that changes the amount of heat delivered — laser wavelength, pulse duration, fluence, or how much melanin is in the treatment area — changes how much it hurts. This is why pain reporting varies so much between studies, devices, and patients, and why it's measurable rather than purely subjective.

Each pulse lasts only milliseconds, so the sensation is brief and repeats as the handpiece moves across the treatment area rather than building into continuous pain. This pulsed, on-off nature is part of why most patients tolerate full sessions without a general anesthetic, even on more sensitive areas.

What Clinical Pain Scores Actually Show

Several controlled trials have measured laser hair removal pain directly using the VAS (a 0–10 or 0–100mm scale where patients mark their perceived pain). In a randomized, split-body trial comparing two diode laser fluence protocols for underarm hair removal, patients rated the low, fixed-fluence protocol at an average of 4.26 out of 10, versus 5.64 out of 10 for a higher, incremental-fluence protocol on the same person's other underarm — a statistically significant difference (P < 0.0003) despite similar hair-reduction results (Pavlović, Adamič & Nenadić, 2015).

A separate comparative study of two diode laser handpieces in Chinese women found first-session immediate pain scores of 5.71 out of 10 for a device with an integrated cooling tip versus 6.86 out of 10 for the same wavelength without that cooling contact — again a statistically significant gap (Zhou, Guo & Gold, 2011). Together, these trials show pain in the 4–7 range on a 10-point scale is typical for underarm treatment, and that settings and equipment meaningfully move that number in either direction. For context, VAS scores in this range are generally classified as mild-to-moderate — comparable to how many people rate a firm eyebrow-threading session or a deep-tissue massage on a sensitive spot, rather than scores of 8–10, which are reserved for severe pain.

Why Some Body Areas Hurt More Than Others

Pain intensity is not evenly distributed across the body, and dermatology consensus attributes this to anatomy rather than the laser itself. Areas with thin skin, dense nerve endings, or close proximity to bone or mucosal tissue tend to register higher pain scores — this is the general clinical pattern reported by practitioners across published case series, even where a single head-to-head, all-areas VAS trial hasn't been run.

In practice, this places areas like the upper lip and the bikini line — including Brazilian treatment — toward the more sensitive end, while larger, thicker-skinned areas such as the legs, arms, and back are typically rated lower. Underarms sit in the middle, which matches the 4–7 range recorded in the trials above. None of this means sensitive areas are unmanageable — it means pain-reduction technique matters more there, covered next.

Individual Factors That Change How Much It Hurts For You

Two patients treating the same body area with the same laser can report different pain scores, and this variation is expected rather than unusual. Coarser, darker hair contains more melanin per follicle, so it absorbs more laser energy and generates more heat than fine, lighter hair — which is also why coarse-haired areas often feel most intense during the first one or two sessions, before hair thins out.

Skin hydration and recent sun exposure also change comfort: sunburned or very dry skin has a lower pain threshold and a higher risk of an exaggerated heat response, which is one reason clinics screen for recent tanning or sunburn before treating. Individual pain threshold itself varies — the same VAS methodology used in the trials above consistently shows a spread of scores around the average, not a single fixed number, because baseline pain sensitivity differs from person to person just as it does for any other procedure.

Dr. Punit Sakhare notes that in clinical practice, patients who are well-hydrated, avoid caffeine immediately before a session, and use a prescribed topical anesthetic on sensitive areas consistently report a more comfortable session than those who skip these steps — a pattern consistent with, though not identical to, what the controlled trials measured.

Pain-Reduction Techniques That Actually Hold Up in Trials

A 2017 systematic review in the Journal of the European Academy of Dermatology and Venereology pooled 32 controlled trials of non-invasive pain-control methods across multiple laser procedures, including nine hair removal studies. It found that active anesthetic methods — topical anesthetic creams, skin cooling, and pneumatic skin flattening (a device that compresses the skin during the pulse) — all outperformed no anesthesia. Topical anesthetics and pneumatic skin flattening showed somewhat better pain reduction than cooling used on its own, though the review rated overall evidence quality as low and called for more standardized head-to-head trials (Greveling, Prens, Liu & van Doorn, 2017).

Topical anesthetic creams have direct trial support in a hair-removal-specific study: a lidocaine 7%/tetracaine 7% combination cream applied 30 minutes before treatment brought average VAS pain down to 3.6 out of 10, compared to 8.4 out of 10 with placebo, in a split-face trial with no significant difference in side effects between groups (Asilian, Shahmoradi, Mazloomi & Nilforoushzadeh, 2014). Combined with the fluence findings above, the clearest evidence-based levers are: a well-fitted topical anesthetic for sensitive areas, an integrated or contact cooling handpiece, and conservative fluence settings rather than pushing energy as high as skin type allows.

Equipment design matters here too, not just technique. The Zhou et al. (2011) comparison isolated cooling-tip design as the sole variable — same 810nm diode wavelength, same operator, same patients — and still found a statistically significant pain difference purely from how the handpiece made contact with skin, because cooling reduces epidermal heat buildup during and immediately after each pulse without meaningfully reducing the heat delivered to the follicle itself. Wavelength also interacts with skin tone and hair thickness in ways that affect comfort, which is closely tied to how side effects vary by skin type — a topic covered in detail in our evidence-based review of laser hair removal side effects by skin type. In short: the laser category matters less for pain than how well the device's cooling and fluence are matched to the individual patient.

Does Laser Hair Removal Get Less Painful Over Multiple Sessions?

Most patients report exactly that, and the mechanism supports it. Laser hair removal only affects follicles in the active growth (anagen) phase at the time of treatment, so each session reduces the density and thickness of hair regrowth rather than eliminating it in one pass. Thinner hair contains less melanin per follicle, meaning less laser energy is absorbed and converted to heat on later sessions — directly lowering the thermal stimulus that causes pain.

This progressive effect is also why the number of sessions needed matters for planning treatment, which we cover separately in how many sessions laser hair removal actually needs, according to the science. Patients who found their first one or two sessions the most uncomfortable, particularly on sensitive areas, typically report each subsequent session feeling noticeably milder.

The Bottom Line

Laser hair removal is not painless, but the clinical evidence puts it in a moderate, tolerable range for most patients and body areas — typically 3–7 out of 10 on a VAS, closer to a rubber-band snap than a burn. Pain is highest on thin-skinned, nerve-dense areas like the upper lip and bikini line and lowest on the legs and back, and it reliably drops across sessions as follicles thin. The techniques with actual trial support — topical anesthetic cream, integrated cooling handpieces, and conservative fluence settings — are exactly what a properly equipped laser hair removal clinic should already be using, so it's worth asking your provider which of these your treatment plan includes.

If you're specifically worried about a sensitive area rather than the procedure overall, that's worth raising directly at consultation — settings, cooling, and anesthetic use can all be adjusted per body area rather than applied uniformly across a full session.
