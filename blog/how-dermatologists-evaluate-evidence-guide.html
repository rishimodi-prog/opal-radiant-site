How Dermatologists Actually Evaluate Evidence: A Plain-English Guide to Reading Aesthetic Medicine Research

By Dr. Punit Sakhare, MD Dermatologist · 2026-08-13 · 9 min read

Quick Facts

Highest-quality evidence
Systematic reviews and meta-analyses that pool multiple randomized controlled trials (RCTs) — not any single study, however impressive.
Lowest-quality evidence
Anecdote, testimonials, and single case reports — useful for generating ideas, not proof a treatment works consistently.
What "peer-reviewed" means
Independent experts checked a study's methods and reasoning before publication. It's a quality floor, not a guarantee the result is definitive.
Biggest red flag
No control group — without one, you can't tell whether the treatment caused the result or it would have happened anyway.
Why sample size matters
A dramatic result from 8 patients can be chance; the same result across 800 patients is far more reliable evidence.

When a clinic says a treatment "works," that claim should be backed by something more specific than confidence. Medicine — including aesthetic and cosmetic dermatology — has a well-established system for weighing evidence: some study designs are far more trustworthy than others, and knowing the difference is the single most useful skill for reading any health claim online, whether it's about lasers, injectables, or supplements.

This guide explains that system in plain English: how evidence gets ranked, what makes a study weak or strong, and the red flags that show up constantly in aesthetic medicine marketing. It's also, deliberately, a look under the hood at how this site's own 30-post evidence library — including this article — is researched, sourced, and clinically reviewed by Dr. Punit Sakhare, MD Dermatologist.

The Evidence Pyramid: Why a Case Report Isn't the Same as a Clinical Trial

Medical evidence isn't binary — "proven" or "not proven." It exists on a hierarchy, often visualized as a pyramid, based on how well each study design controls for bias and chance. At the bottom sit anecdote and expert opinion: an individual's experience or a clinician's general impression, useful for generating ideas but not proof anything works consistently. One level up is the case report or case series — a detailed writeup of what happened to one patient, or a handful, with no comparison group.

Higher up are observational studies — cohort studies (following a group over time) and case-control studies (comparing people with an outcome to people without it) — which can find associations but can't fully rule out confounding factors, like patients who chose a treatment also tending to have healthier skin habits overall. Near the top sits the randomized controlled trial (RCT): participants are randomly assigned to receive the treatment or a comparison (placebo, an alternative treatment, or no treatment), which is the single most powerful tool for showing a treatment — not chance, not the passage of time, not a patient's expectations — actually caused an outcome. At the very top sits the systematic review or meta-analysis, which pools and critically appraises multiple RCTs to produce the most reliable estimate available for a given question.

This ordering isn't academic snobbery. A single dramatic case report can be entirely true and still be unrepresentative; a well-run RCT with hundreds of patients tells you what to expect on average, which is what actually matters when deciding whether to book a treatment.

What "Peer-Reviewed" Actually Means — and Its Limits

Peer review is the process by which a journal sends a submitted study to independent experts in the same field, who assess whether the methods are sound, the statistics are handled correctly, and the conclusions actually follow from the data — before the journal agrees to publish it. It exists specifically to catch the kind of errors and overreach that are easy to miss when you're the one who ran the study.

But peer review is a floor, not a ceiling. It doesn't guarantee a result will replicate, and it doesn't catch every flaw — reviewers work from what's disclosed to them, not from raw data or the lab itself. Journal quality also varies enormously: reputable dermatology journals indexed on PubMed/MEDLINE (the U.S. National Library of Medicine's database) apply meaningfully more scrutiny than "predatory" journals, which will publish almost anything for a fee with minimal or no genuine review. PubMed indexing is a reasonable quality signal — it means a study met a journal's editorial and methodological bar — but it isn't a guarantee that any single paper is the final word. That's why this guide treats systematic reviews and multiple converging studies as stronger evidence than any one paper alone.

Why Sample Size and Study Design Change What a Result Means

Sample size — how many participants a study includes — determines how much you can trust its result. A study on 8 patients can show what looks like a dramatic 80% improvement rate purely by chance; a study on 800 patients showing the same 80% is a far more stable, generalizable number. Statisticians express this uncertainty as a confidence interval — a range around the reported result that reflects how much it might shift with a different sample. Small studies have wide confidence intervals, meaning the "true" effect could be much smaller (or larger) than the headline number suggests.

Study design changes what a result can prove. A control group — a comparison arm that doesn't receive the active treatment — is what lets researchers separate a treatment's real effect from what would have happened anyway: skin naturally has good and bad days, redness fades on its own, and the human eye is prone to noticing what it expects to see, particularly in unblinded before/after photos taken by someone with a financial interest in the outcome. Blinding — keeping patients and/or assessors unaware of which group someone is in — further reduces the chance that expectation, not the treatment, drives the reported result. Without a control group and some form of blinding, "it worked" and "it would have happened anyway" are very hard to tell apart.

Red Flags: How to Spot Weak Evidence in Aesthetic Medicine Marketing

Once you know what strong evidence looks like, weak evidence becomes easier to spot — especially in aesthetic medicine, where marketing budgets often outpace research budgets. Common red flags include:

  • No control group. Before-and-after photos alone show a change over time, not that the treatment caused it.
  • A single case report presented as general proof. "This patient saw dramatic results" is a data point, not a conclusion about what most people should expect.
  • Funded and run entirely by the device manufacturer, with no independent replication. Industry funding doesn't automatically invalidate a study, but a result that's never been reproduced by an unaffiliated research group deserves more scrutiny — especially when the sponsor also owns the device being tested.
  • No mention of adverse events. A study reporting only satisfaction scores, and never discussing complications, dropouts, or side effects, is telling you half the story.
  • Vague "improvement" claims with no validated measurement. Credible studies use objective scoring tools — standardized scar scales, colorimeter readings, blinded photo-panel review; "patients felt their skin looked better" without a defined scale is much weaker evidence.
  • Testimonials used in place of data. A testimonial is marketing, not a study — it has no comparison group, no measurement, and is selected for being persuasive, not representative.

The Aesthetic Medicine Evidence Problem — Why This Field Needs Extra Scrutiny

It's worth being honest about a real, well-documented pattern: aesthetic and cosmetic dermatology, as a field, generally has fewer large randomized controlled trials than other areas of medicine. A 2021 systematic review of non-surgical rhinoplasty using hyaluronic acid dermal fillers — screening nearly 2,900 citations — could locate only 11 studies rigorous enough to include, and explicitly concluded there was "a paucity of quality data in the form of experimental and prospective studies" (Kumar et al., 2021). A separate systematic review of fractional CO2 laser treatment for hypertrophic scars found that while results across five randomized trials were promising, several studies were uncontrolled with short follow-up periods, and the authors called for "controlled comparative studies... with larger sample size and longer follow-up period" (Simbolon Sitohang et al., 2021). A 2024 review of platelet-rich plasma (PRP) in aesthetic dermatology found consistently positive reported outcomes but "significant heterogeneity in PRP preparation methods and treatment protocols," and called for standardized, large-scale trials before the evidence base can be considered settled (Asubiaro & Avajah, 2024).

Part of this is structural: many aesthetic devices reach the market through regulatory pathways (like the FDA's 510(k) clearance) that require showing a device is substantially similar to one already cleared, not that it has passed a large efficacy trial the way a new drug would. Part of it is that a smaller field has a smaller pool of independent research funding, and a larger share of what does exist is industry-sponsored — not disqualifying on its own, but a reason to weight independent replication more heavily. None of this means aesthetic treatments don't work; the underlying mechanisms behind lasers, RF devices, and hyaluronic acid fillers are well understood biologically. It means claims should be sized to the actual evidence — "the mechanism is well established and early trials are promising" is a more honest sentence than "clinically proven," and it's the sentence this content library tries to write.
