Is It Safe to Get Laser Hair Removal During Your Period? What the Research Says

By Dr. Punit Sakhare, MD Dermatologist · 2026-07-18 · 7 min read

Quick Facts

Is it safe?
Yes. Menstruation doesn't appear on recognised contraindication lists for laser hair removal — pregnancy, active infection, recent tan, photosensitising medication and keloid history are the actual exclusions.
Does it hurt more?
The most methodologically rigorous study found no significant menstrual-phase effect on most experimental pain measures in healthy women.
What about heat pain specifically?
A controlled study found thermal pain sensitivity — the closest match to a laser's heat sensation — did not vary significantly across the menstrual cycle, even though a different pain type (ischemic pain) did.
Women vs. men, generally
In an actual laser hair removal pain study, female patients reported lower pain scores than male patients for the same Nd:YAG treatment.
The honest caveat
Some women do report feeling more generally tender before their period. That's a real, valid reason to reschedule for comfort — it just hasn't shown up as a strong, consistent signal in controlled pain-threshold research.

"Can I still come in for my session if I'm on my period?" is one of the most common questions we get booking laser hair removal appointments — understandably, since period-related tenderness and sensitivity are real, common experiences. The short answer is yes, it's safe, and the pain research on this specific question is more reassuring than most people expect. Here's what it actually says, with the caveats that are worth knowing too.

Is There Any Safety Reason to Avoid Laser During Your Period?

No. The laser targets melanin in the hair follicle — a mechanism entirely unrelated to the uterine lining shedding during menstruation. At OPAL Radiant, as at most reputable clinics, the actual contraindications we screen for before any laser hair removal session are pregnancy or breastfeeding, an active skin infection or open wound in the treatment area, a recent tan or sunburn, use of photosensitising medication such as isotretinoin, a history of keloid scarring, and age under 16. Menstruation isn't on that list, and there's no mechanism by which it would need to be — the treatment area's skin and hair follicles aren't affected by where you are in your cycle.

What About Pain — Does Your Cycle Actually Change How Much It Hurts?

This is the part with genuine research behind it, and it's more nuanced — and more reassuring — than the common belief that periods reliably make everything hurt more. A study published in the Clinical Journal of Pain was specifically designed to correct the limitations of earlier menstrual-cycle pain research — small sample sizes, no ovulation verification, single pain measures — by testing 41 healthy, regularly cycling women across mechanical, electrocutaneous and ischemic pain measures in both the mid-follicular and late-luteal (premenstrual) phases. The result: no significant menstrual-phase effect on almost any pain outcome tested, with a single exception — electrocutaneous pain threshold was somewhat lower (meaning slightly more sensitive) in the late-luteal phase. The authors concluded that hormonal variation across the cycle "has a minimal effect on subjective and physiological responses to pain" (Bartley & Rhudy, 2013).

An earlier study in Psychosomatic Medicine looked specifically at whether different types of pain respond differently to cycle phase, testing both thermal (heat) and ischemic pain across the midfollicular, ovulatory, and premenstrual phases. It found greater ischemic pain sensitivity around ovulation and premenstrually — but thermal pain responses "did not vary significantly across menstrual cycle phases" (Fillingim et al., 1997). This distinction matters directly for laser hair removal: the sensation from a laser pulse is fundamentally a heat-based, thermal sensation, not the deep, cramping ischemic-type pain that showed cycle variation in this research. On the specific pain modality most relevant to a laser session, the evidence points to no meaningful cycle effect.

Why the "Thermal Pain" Distinction Matters Here

Laser hair removal devices work by heating melanin in the hair follicle to disable it — the discomfort patients feel is a brief thermal (heat) sensation, sometimes described as a warm snap, not a deep aching or cramping pain. That's exactly the pain category the Psychosomatic Medicine study found to be stable across the menstrual cycle, while the pain type that did show some cycle-linked variation (ischemia, the kind of pain from restricted blood flow, similar to menstrual cramps themselves) isn't the mechanism at play during a laser session (Fillingim et al., 1997). In other words, the specific type of discomfort periods are well known to intensify — cramping, ischemic-type pain — isn't the type of discomfort a laser produces.

The Honest Caveat: Subjective Experience vs. Measured Thresholds

None of this means every woman experiences her cycle identically, and it's worth being upfront about a genuinely interesting nuance in the research. A study comparing women with premenstrual syndrome (PMS) to a non-PMS control group found no significant difference in measured pain threshold or pain tolerance between the groups across cycle phases — but the PMS group rated their pain intensity as higher overall, "irrespective of menstrual cycle phase" (Kuczmierczyk & Adams, 1986). The authors point to cognitive and perceptual factors, not necessarily a change in the raw physical pain signal itself, as part of the explanation. Practically, this means some women's lived experience of feeling "more sensitive" around their period is real and valid, even in the absence of a large, consistent shift in laboratory-measured pain thresholds across the broader population.

It's also worth noting that women generally tolerate laser hair removal well compared to men: in a clinical trial comparing two topical anaesthetics for Nd:YAG laser hair removal, female patients reported significantly lower pain scores than male patients undergoing the identical treatment, regardless of cycle timing (Guardiano & Norwood, 2005).

What This Means in Practice

If you're due for a session and your period arrives, there's no safety reason to cancel, and the strongest pain research available suggests it's unlikely to meaningfully change how the session feels — particularly since laser discomfort is a thermal sensation, the specific pain type shown not to vary across the cycle. That said, if you personally feel more tender or sensitive premenstrually, that's a legitimate, individual reason to reschedule for your own comfort — you know your body better than a population-level study does. For bikini or Brazilian area sessions specifically, using a tampon or menstrual cup rather than a pad during your appointment is a simple practical accommodation, and our team is used to working around this — just mention it when you book.

The Bottom Line

There is no dermatological safety reason to avoid laser hair removal during your period, and the best-designed pain research available finds little to no menstrual-cycle effect on most pain measures — including, specifically, the thermal pain sensation most relevant to how a laser actually feels (Bartley & Rhudy, 2013; Fillingim et al., 1997). If you'd still rather reschedule for personal comfort, that's a perfectly reasonable choice too — just call your clinic and we'll move your appointment.
