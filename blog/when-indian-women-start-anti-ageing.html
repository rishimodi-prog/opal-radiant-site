Anti-Ageing

When Should Indian Women Start Anti-Ageing Treatments?

By Dr. Punit Sakhare, MD Dermatologist · 2026-06-27 · 5 min read

One question I hear often: "Am I too young to start anti-ageing treatments?" Or "Am I too late?" The answer depends on your skin, genetics, and lifestyle — not just age. Here's a decade-by-decade guide for Indian women.

The 20s: Prevention Foundation

Your 20s are for prevention, not correction. Focus on: daily SPF 50+ — sun protection is the single most evidence-backed anti-ageing habit, and in a 4.5-year randomised controlled trial, adults who used sunscreen daily showed 24% less visible photoageing than those who applied it only at their own discretion (Hughes et al., 2013); retinol at night, starting low (around 0.1%) and building slowly — even low-dose retinol has been shown in a placebo-controlled study to improve fine lines and skin tone without significant irritation (Bellemère et al., 2009); vitamin C serum in the morning; and adequate sleep and hydration. In-clinic: monthly Hydra Facial for hydration, quarterly light peels for texture. Don't start injectables or aggressive lasers unless you have a specific concern.

The 30s: Early Correction

Collagen production declines measurably with age — foundational dermal biopsy research established that skin collagen content falls fairly steadily through adulthood (Shuster et al., 1975), which is where the commonly cited estimate of roughly 1% collagen loss per year comes from. In practice, you'll start noticing subtle changes. Fine lines around eyes and mouth. Slight loss of jawline definition. Uneven texture. Start: consistent MNRF for collagen (annual course), quarterly peels for texture and pigment, targeted retinoid strength (0.5–1%), preventive Botox for expressive lines (if desired). Skip: aggressive fillers, unnecessary "prejuvenation" trends.

The 40s: Active Rejuvenation

The decade of visible ageing accelerates: jawline sagging, deeper lines, volume loss, more pigment. This is when non-invasive lifting becomes valuable. Start: HIFU for jawline and jowl definition, MNRF regularly, targeted fillers for tear troughs or cheek volume (if needed), professional-grade home care (prescription retinol, brightening actives). Consider: annual maintenance HIFU, biannual MNRF courses.

The 50s: Comprehensive Care

Menopause accelerates ageing (dry skin, further collagen loss). Your plan combines lifting (HIFU), collagen building (MNRF), volume (fillers where needed), and skin quality (peels, home care). Some women also consider hormone replacement discussion with their gynaecologist — this affects skin quality significantly.

The 60s+: Maintenance and Quality of Life

Continue what works. Add hydration-focused treatments (Hydra Facial, LED). Skin-quality-focused topicals. Regular dermatologist reviews for skin cancer screening in Indian skin (often overlooked).

Regardless of Age

Sun protection is the single biggest anti-ageing intervention. Adequate sleep, hydration, and stress management matter more than most treatments. Consistency over intensity — regular small interventions beat occasional aggressive ones.

When to See a Dermatologist

Sooner than you think. A dermatologist consultation in your late 20s establishes a plan, catches issues early, and prevents wasted spending on products/treatments you don't need.

The Realistic Truth

Ageing is inevitable. Anti-ageing treatments delay and soften it, but don't stop it. The goal isn't to look 20 forever — it's to look like your best self at every age.
