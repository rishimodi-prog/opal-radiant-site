Melasma

Can Melasma Be Cured Permanently? What Recurrence Studies Actually Show

By Dr. Punit Sakhare, MD Dermatologist · 2026-07-27 · 8 min read

Quick Facts

The short answer
No treatment currently available reliably cures melasma permanently — it is classified in the dermatology literature as a chronic, relapsing pigmentary disorder.
Why it relapses
Melasma is driven by hyperactive, UV- and hormone-sensitive melanocytes that switch back on when triggers (sun, heat, hormones) return, even after pigment is cleared.
Recurrence after triple combination cream
In a 2024 split-face trial, participants treated with triple combination cream showed significantly more pigment recurrence after stopping treatment than those on a gentler maintenance regimen (Tantanasrigul et al., 2024).
Recurrence after oral tranexamic acid
A 2023 randomized trial found low-dose oral tranexamic acid maintained results well at 12 weeks post-treatment, with relapse rates described as "very low" when tapered rather than stopped abruptly (Bhattacharjee et al., 2023).
What actually reduces relapse
Daily broad-spectrum sunscreen, ongoing low-strength maintenance topicals, and avoiding known triggers — not a single procedure — are what the evidence links to durable control.

"Can melasma be cured permanently?" is the question we hear most often at consultation, usually from someone who has already tried at least one cream or peel that worked — for a while. It's a fair question, and the honest, evidence-based answer is more nuanced than a yes or no: melasma currently has no permanent cure, but it does have highly effective, durable control strategies, and the published research on recurrence rates explains exactly why the distinction matters.

This article is deliberately narrow. If you want the full picture of what causes melasma and how it's treated, we cover that in Understanding Melasma: Causes, Triggers, and Treatments. Here, we're answering one specific question with the recurrence and relapse data behind it.

Why Dermatology Classifies Melasma as "Chronic Relapsing," Not Curable

In the medical literature, melasma is consistently described with a specific phrase: a chronic relapsing pigmentary disorder. A 2022 treatment review in Dermatology and Therapy states this directly — noting that "although there is currently no curative treatment available for melasma," photoprotection and combination therapy remain the main strategies for control and prevention (Cassiano et al., 2022). That single phrase, no curative treatment available, is the clinical consensus reached after decades of studying topical, oral, and procedural treatments, not a marketing caveat added to manage expectations.

The distinction that matters here is between clearing pigment and curing the underlying condition. Treatments can very effectively reduce or eliminate visible pigment in the short term — modified Melasma Area and Severity Index (mMASI) scores, the standard way researchers measure melasma severity, routinely drop by 50 to 90 percent in published trials. What they don't do is permanently switch off the melanocyte behavior that produces melasma in the first place. That same 2022 review notes that because melasma's pathogenesis isn't yet fully understood, current strategies mostly work by reducing melanin synthesis and helping the skin clear existing pigment, rather than correcting whatever keeps the melanocytes primed to overreact (Cassiano et al., 2022).

This matters for how a treatment plan should be framed from the start. A plan built around "clear it once and you're done" sets up disappointment when pigment returns months later — not because the treatment failed, but because relapse is the expected biological behavior of the condition, documented across published cohorts, not a sign that something went wrong.

What Actually Relapses: The Biology in Plain English

Melasma forms when melanocytes (the pigment-producing cells in skin) in sun-exposed areas become chronically overactive, triggered by a combination of ultraviolet exposure, visible light, heat, and hormonal signals (estrogen and progesterone in particular). Treatment — whether a topical cream, a peel, or a laser — works by reducing the pigment these cells have already deposited and, to varying degrees, calming their activity for a period of time.

What treatment does not do is permanently alter the melanocytes' underlying sensitivity to those triggers. A global survey by the Pigmentary Disorders Academy, which questioned 324 women being treated for melasma across nine clinics worldwide, found that pregnancy, hormonal contraception, family history, and sun exposure interact in ways that both trigger onset and shape how the condition recurs over time — for instance, women with a family history of melasma tended to have darker baseline skin tone and different onset patterns than those without one (Ortonne et al., 2009). As long as a trigger (sun exposure, heat, hormonal fluctuation) is present and the melanocytes retain that heightened sensitivity, pigment can return — which is exactly what recurrence studies observe once treatment stops.

This is also why melasma behaves differently from a simple sunspot or a single patch of post-inflammatory pigmentation: those forms of pigmentation typically involve a one-time or time-limited insult to the skin, while melasma involves a durable shift in how a defined population of melanocytes responds to ordinary daily exposures. Removing the visible pigment doesn't reverse that underlying shift.

What Recurrence Actually Looks Like After Stopping Treatment

The clearest way to answer "can it be cured" is to look at what happens when treatment stops. A 2024 split-face, evaluator-blinded randomized study compared triple combination cream (TCC) — often called the gold-standard topical for melasma — against a gentler alpha-arbutin and kojic acid combination, followed by a 4-week observation period after both treatments were discontinued. The TCC side, despite showing stronger initial improvement, showed significantly higher recurrence severity after discontinuation than the arbutin/kojic acid side, measured by both melanin index and severity score (Tantanasrigul et al., 2024).

This is a pattern seen across melasma treatment types, not just topicals: whatever clears pigment fastest often relapses fastest once stopped, because it hasn't changed the melanocytes' underlying reactivity — only removed the pigment they had already made. It's a strong argument for treatment plans built around sustainable maintenance rather than a single aggressive course.

What the Oral Tranexamic Acid Data Shows About Maintenance

Oral tranexamic acid (TXA) is one of the more researched systemic options for melasma, and its relapse data is informative precisely because researchers have studied dosing and tapering strategies rather than just a single stop-and-check design. A 2023 randomized trial comparing two oral TXA dosing regimens (250 mg vs. 500 mg, twice daily, for 12 weeks) found that both groups maintained their improvement well through 12 weeks of follow-up after stopping treatment, with the authors reporting relapse rates as "very low" in both arms — and concluding that not all patients require high-dose or extended maintenance dosing to sustain results (Bhattacharjee et al., 2023). Notably, the lower 250 mg dose performed comparably to the higher 500 mg dose on both pigment reduction and relapse, suggesting that more aggressive dosing isn't what determines whether results hold.

That's a meaningfully different relapse pattern from the triple combination cream discontinuation data above, and it illustrates the broader point running through this entire body of research: relapse rates are not fixed or uniform — they depend heavily on which treatment was used, how it was tapered versus stopped abruptly, and whether sun protection and low-level maintenance therapy continued afterward. None of the published trials on oral TXA, topical combinations, or laser-based treatments report a relapse-free outcome once every intervention is withdrawn; what varies is how much and how fast pigment returns, and that variation tracks closely with how the taper and maintenance phase was handled.

So What Does Reduce Recurrence? The Evidence-Backed Maintenance Approach

Given that no single procedure eliminates melasma permanently, the research consistently points to an ongoing maintenance strategy rather than a one-time fix:

  • Daily broad-spectrum sun protection — including protection against visible light, not just UV, since visible light also triggers melasma-prone melanocytes. Photoprotection is described across the treatment literature as the foundational, non-negotiable step underneath every other intervention (Cassiano et al., 2022).
  • Lower-strength maintenance topicals between active treatment courses, rather than stopping all products abruptly — consistent with the lower recurrence seen with the gentler regimen in the 2024 split-face trial (Tantanasrigul et al., 2024).
  • Tapered rather than abrupt discontinuation of systemic treatments like oral TXA, which the dosing-comparison trial associated with very low relapse compared to stopping outright (Bhattacharjee et al., 2023).
  • Trigger awareness — hormonal contraception, pregnancy, multiple pregnancies, and prolonged outdoor sun exposure are all documented drivers of both onset and recurrence, with risk of onset during pregnancy rising with each additional pregnancy in the global survey data (Ortonne et al., 2009). If melasma appeared or worsened during pregnancy, the pattern of triggers and recurrence can differ — see Melasma in Pregnancy: Understanding Chloasma for that specific case.
  • Realistic treatment spacing — because relapse is expected rather than exceptional, many dermatologists plan periodic "touch-up" sessions or short maintenance courses into a long-term calendar from the outset, rather than treating recurrence as a failure that requires a completely new approach.

What This Means for Setting Expectations

None of this means treatment isn't worth pursuing — the same studies that document recurrence also document real, measurable improvement, often substantial, with appropriate treatment. What it means is that the honest framing isn't "cure" but durable control: getting pigment down significantly, then maintaining that result with sustained sun protection and lower-intensity upkeep, rather than expecting one course of treatment to be the end of it.

Dr. Punit Sakhare's clinical approach at Opal Radiant reflects this: treatment plans are built with a maintenance phase from the outset, not added on only after a relapse. Patients who understand going in that melasma is a manageable, not curable, condition tend to have more realistic expectations and better long-term satisfaction with results.

The Bottom Line

Based on the published recurrence data, melasma cannot currently be cured permanently — it is a chronic condition that responds well to treatment but tends to relapse when treatment and sun protection stop, because the underlying melanocyte sensitivity to UV, visible light, and hormones isn't eliminated by any current therapy. The practical takeaway from the evidence is straightforward: pair active treatment with a realistic, ongoing maintenance plan, and recurrence can be minimized substantially, even if it can't be guaranteed away entirely.
