Sunscreen for Indian Skin: How Much SPF Do You Actually Need?

By Dr. Punit Sakhare, MD Dermatologist · 2026-07-30 · 9 min read

Quick Facts

Minimum SPF
SPF 30-50 for daily wear; higher does not mean proportionally more protection past SPF 50.
What SPF actually measures
Protection against UVB (the burning ray) only — it says nothing about UVA protection on its own.
What PA measures
UVA protection, graded PA+ to PA++++ — the rating that matters most for pigmentation, not just sunburn.
Most Indian skin is
Fitzpatrick III-V, which tans and pigments more readily than it burns — meaning UVA exposure shows up as dark spots, not redness.
Why this matters for melasma
UVA1 and visible light — not just UVB — are established drivers of melasma and post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation in darker skin (Morgado-Carrasco et al., 2022).
Best format for pigment-prone skin
Tinted, iron-oxide-containing broad-spectrum sunscreen, which adds visible-light protection that plain white sunscreens don't provide.

"Just wear sunscreen" is common advice, but it skips the actual question most people in India have: how much SPF, what does PA mean, and does any of this change if you already have melasma or dark spots? The answer depends on skin physiology that's specific to Fitzpatrick III-V skin — the range most Indian skin falls into — and on a part of the light spectrum that plain SPF numbers don't even measure. This article covers what SPF and PA ratings actually test, why Indian skin's pigment response changes the calculation, and why sunscreen is described as "step one" in managing melasma rather than an optional add-on.

SPF Only Tells Half the Story

SPF (Sun Protection Factor) is a lab-tested measure of how much longer skin takes to redden under UVB — the shorter-wavelength ultraviolet ray primarily responsible for sunburn — when a sunscreen is applied versus bare skin. SPF 30 filters out roughly 97% of UVB rays; SPF 50 filters out about 98%. The jump from 30 to 50 is real but small, and no sunscreen blocks 100% of UVB regardless of the number on the bottle. It's also a laboratory figure measured at a thick, evenly spread application (2 mg/cm²) that most people don't actually apply in daily life, which is part of why real-world protection tends to run lower than the label number suggests.

What SPF does not measure is UVA protection. UVA rays are longer-wavelength, penetrate deeper into the dermis, and are present at consistent intensity throughout daylight hours year-round — including through window glass and on cloudy days — unlike UVB, which peaks around midday. A sunscreen can carry a high SPF number and still offer weak UVA protection unless it's specifically formulated and tested as broad-spectrum. This is not a minor technicality: UVA is the wavelength most implicated in the pigmentary changes covered later in this article, so a sunscreen chosen on SPF alone can leave the exact risk that matters most for Indian skin only partially covered.

PA Rating: The Number Indian Sunscreen Labels Should Have (and Often Do)

PA (Protection Grade of UVA) is the rating system used across most Indian and Asian sunscreen labels, graded PA+ through PA++++ based on the Persistent Pigment Darkening test. Each "+" represents a step up in UVA protection: PA++++ indicates the highest tested category. For daily use in India's UV climate, dermatology guidance generally favors a sunscreen labeled both SPF 30-50+ and PA+++ or PA++++ — the SPF number alone doesn't confirm the UVA side is covered.

This distinction matters more in India than in lower-UV-index countries, because India's latitude keeps UVA and UVB intensity high for most of the year, and because — as covered below — the skin response to UVA in Fitzpatrick III-V skin tends to be pigmentary rather than just a sunburn risk.

Why Fitzpatrick Skin Type Changes the Calculation

The Fitzpatrick scale (I-VI) classifies skin by how it responds to UV exposure — burning versus tanning tendency — and most Indian skin falls in the Fitzpatrick III-V range, with some individuals at VI. This has a direct practical consequence: Fitzpatrick III-V skin has more baseline epidermal melanin, so it burns less easily than Fitzpatrick I-II skin but pigments — tans, darkens, and forms dark spots — more readily and more visibly in response to the same UV dose. How Fitzpatrick typing is assessed and why it matters for treatment planning is covered in more detail separately, but the sunscreen implication is straightforward: "I don't burn easily" is not a reason to skip or under-apply sunscreen in Indian skin — it's a reason to prioritize UVA coverage specifically, because pigmentary damage can accumulate without any sunburn warning sign at all.

This is a genuinely different risk profile from the one most global sunscreen marketing is built around. Sunburn is a visible, short-term signal that tells lighter skin types when they've had too much sun; Fitzpatrick III-V skin often doesn't get that signal at the same UV dose, even though the underlying UV and visible-light exposure is still triggering melanocyte activity beneath the surface. The absence of redness can create a false sense that unprotected sun exposure was harmless, when the actual outcome — gradual tanning, uneven tone, or a slowly darkening patch of melasma — simply takes longer to become visible.

UVA's Specific Role in Melasma and Post-Inflammatory Hyperpigmentation

Melasma — a patchy, symmetric facial hyperpigmentation that disproportionately affects darker-skinned populations, reported in an estimated 30-50% of South American and Asian women in some cohorts — is now understood as more than a UVB problem. A 2022 narrative review in Photodermatology, Photoimmunology & Photomedicine concluded that long-wave UVA (UVA1) and high-energy visible light (HEVL) play a key role in melasma pathophysiology, alongside genetics and sex hormones, and that melasma shares physiological features with photoaging (Morgado-Carrasco et al., 2022). The same review found that dark-skinned patients tend to take fewer photoprotective measures despite higher risk, and that consistent, intensive use of broad-spectrum sunscreen can prevent melasma in high-risk individuals, reduce its severity, and lower relapse rates.

Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) — dark marks that persist after acne, a breakout, or a clinical procedure — follows a related mechanism: inflamed skin overproduces melanin in response to UV and visible light exposure during healing, which is why sun protection is emphasized as aftercare following treatments like chemical peels or carbon laser facials, when skin is temporarily more reactive to light-triggered pigment changes.

Why Tinted, Iron-Oxide Sunscreens Are Specifically Recommended for Pigment-Prone Skin

Standard mineral and chemical sunscreens filter UVA and UVB but generally do not block visible light, including HEVL — the portion of the spectrum a 2022 review identified as a melasma trigger alongside UVA1 (Morgado-Carrasco et al., 2022). Iron oxides, the pigments used in tinted sunscreens, physically scatter and absorb visible light in addition to the UV filters doing their usual job, which is why tinted formulations are recommended specifically for melasma and other pigmentary concerns rather than as a cosmetic afterthought.

This isn't a fringe opinion. A 2024 survey of 221 dermatologists and dermatology residents in Spain found that when treating patients with skin of color and pigmentary disorders, 96.8% prioritized UVA protection and 59.5% specifically recommended iron-oxide-containing sunscreens, alongside near-universal preference for SPF 30+ (Morgado-Carrasco et al., 2024). Separately, an international consensus panel using the RAND/UCLA appropriateness method rated tinted sunscreen with iron oxide as appropriate across all Fitzpatrick skin types, noting it was particularly relevant for darker phototypes (Fitzpatrick IV-VI) to achieve visible-light protection that closely matches diverse skin tones (Draelos et al., 2024).

Does Sunscreen Actually Prevent Pigmentation? The Clinical Trial Data

Beyond the mechanistic argument, there's direct interventional evidence. A 12-month clinical trial of 200 pregnant women — a population with a very high baseline rate of melasma (chloasma) due to hormonal changes — tested a broad-spectrum sunscreen (SPF 50+, UVA-PF 28) as the sole preventive intervention. Only 2.7% of participants developed new chloasma, compared with a 53% incidence previously observed in a similar population without the intervention; participants with pre-existing chloasma also showed measurable lightening by study end (Lakhdar et al., 2007). While this trial is older and specific to pregnancy-related melasma, it's one of the few controlled looks at sunscreen as prevention rather than just theory, and the magnitude of the difference supports the mechanistic case made by more recent reviews.

Practical Application: What This Means Day to Day

Translating the evidence into a routine comes down to a few points dermatology sources converge on:

  • Choose SPF 30-50 with PA+++ or PA++++ — check both numbers, not just SPF, since a high SPF alone does not confirm meaningful UVA coverage.
  • Apply generously and reapply roughly every 3-4 hours of continuous sun exposure, since sunscreen degrades, rubs off, and is typically under-applied relative to the amount used in lab SPF testing; a single thin morning layer rarely lasts a full day outdoors.
  • Consider a tinted, iron-oxide formula if you have melasma, PIH, or are undergoing pigment-sensitive treatments, since it adds visible-light coverage plain sunscreen lacks.
  • Don't skip it because you "don't burn" — Fitzpatrick III-V skin's lower burn tendency doesn't mean lower pigmentation risk; it often means the opposite.
  • Apply it indoors too, not just outdoors — UVA passes through window glass, so time spent near windows in a car, office, or home still counts as UVA exposure even without direct sun.
  • Treat sunscreen as step one, not an extra step, alongside professional treatment. In melasma management, consistent sun protection is the foundation every other treatment is layered onto — active ingredients and in-clinic procedures underperform without it, because ongoing UVA and visible-light exposure keeps re-triggering the same pigment pathway.

None of this requires an expensive or elaborate routine — it requires matching the label to the risk (broad-spectrum, adequate PA, tinted if pigment-prone) and applying it consistently enough that protection doesn't lapse for hours at a time.
