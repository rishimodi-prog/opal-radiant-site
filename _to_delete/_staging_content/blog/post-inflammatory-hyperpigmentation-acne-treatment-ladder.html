Pigmentation

Post-Inflammatory Hyperpigmentation After Acne: An Evidence-Based Treatment Ladder for Indian Skin

By Dr. Punit Sakhare, MD Dermatologist · 2026-08-07 · 9 min read

Quick Facts

What it is
Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) is excess melanin left behind after an acne lesion heals — it is a scar-like mark, not active acne, and not a true pigment disorder like melasma.
Who gets it worse
Fitzpatrick skin types IV–VI (most Indian skin) have more reactive melanocytes and develop darker, longer-lasting PIH than lighter skin after the same degree of inflammation.
How long it lasts untreated
Epidermal PIH can fade over 6–12 months; dermal PIH (pigment that has dropped into the deeper skin layer) can persist for years without treatment.
First-line topicals
Azelaic acid, niacinamide, and retinoids are the best-tolerated starting agents for Indian skin; hydroquinone is more potent but needs dermatologist supervision.
When procedures help
Superficial chemical peels and conservative-setting Q-switched or picosecond lasers can accelerate clearance once topicals plateau — but overly aggressive settings can trigger more PIH in darker skin.
The one non-negotiable
Daily broad-spectrum sunscreen — UV and even visible light re-stimulate the same pigment-producing pathway and can undo months of treatment.

Almost everyone who has had acne knows this pattern: the pimple heals, but a flat brown or grey-brown mark stays behind for weeks or months. This is post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) — and it disproportionately affects Fitzpatrick skin types IV–VI, which includes the majority of Indian skin tones. According to PubMed, a 2026 review notes that patients with skin of colour have an increased risk of PIH and often present with more pronounced, longer-lasting dyspigmentation than patients with lighter skin (Yadav et al., 2026). This article lays out what actually causes PIH, why darker skin reacts this way, and a stepwise treatment ladder — from daily topicals through procedural options — grounded in published dermatology literature rather than marketing claims.

What PIH Actually Is (and What It Isn't)

PIH is not a scar in the textural sense, and it is not the same condition as melasma. It is a flat, discoloured mark that forms when inflammation from an acne lesion triggers melanocytes — the pigment-producing cells in skin — to over-produce melanin in and around the site of injury. The mark itself contains no active infection or inflammation; it is the aftermath.

Clinically, PIH is graded by both depth and severity. According to PubMed, a five-expert consensus review proposed an easy-to-use severity grading tool specifically for acne-induced PIH (termed AI-PIH), noting that pigmentation can occur even after mild-to-moderate acne without visibly dramatic inflammation, and that the condition is mainly observed in patients with Fitzpatrick phototypes IV to VI (Auffret et al., 2025). This matters clinically: two patients with the same acne severity can end up with very different pigmentation outcomes depending on their baseline skin phototype and how the lesion was managed (or picked at) during healing.

The Pathophysiology: Why Darker Skin Reacts More Strongly

The mechanism starts with inflammation. When a pimple becomes inflamed, keratinocytes (the main cells of the outer skin layer) and immune cells release cytokines and growth factors that signal nearby melanocytes to ramp up melanin production and transfer it to surrounding skin cells — a process sometimes summarised as inflammation-triggered melanogenesis. According to PubMed, a 2026 review describes this as a complex, multi-pathway process involving ultraviolet exposure, local skin inflammation, and cytokine/growth-factor signalling between keratinocytes, melanocytes, and dermal fibroblasts (Yadav et al., 2026).

In Fitzpatrick IV–VI skin, melanocytes are constitutively more reactive — they are primed to produce more melanin per unit of inflammatory signal than melanocytes in lighter skin types. That is the core reason the same acne lesion that leaves faint, fast-fading redness in fair skin can leave a dark, months-long mark in Indian skin. When melanin is deposited only in the epidermis (outer layer), it tends to resolve faster; when it drops into the dermis (deeper layer) via a process called pigment incontinence, it becomes far more stubborn and can persist for years without active treatment (Yadav et al., 2026).

Ongoing exposure keeps the cycle going. Sunlight and even visible light can independently stimulate melanocytes and worsen existing PIH — which is why photoprotection is treated as foundational, not optional, in every published management framework for this condition.

Step 1: Prevention and Photoprotection — Before Any Active Treatment

Before layering on actives, the treatment ladder starts with stopping new pigment from forming. That means treating the underlying acne itself (untreated or picked-at acne generates more inflammation and therefore more PIH), and applying broad-spectrum sunscreen daily, reapplied through the day. According to PubMed, published guidance identifies photoprotection as the cornerstone of PIH management given the photosensitive nature of the condition, recommended alongside every other topical, light-based, or peel treatment rather than instead of them (Yadav et al., 2026).

Patients are also advised against picking or squeezing acne lesions, since mechanical trauma is itself a potent inflammatory trigger that predictably produces more PIH than a lesion left to resolve on its own.

Step 2: First-Line Topicals — Azelaic Acid, Niacinamide, and Retinoids

Azelaic acid is often the best starting point for Indian skin because it treats active acne and pigmentation simultaneously, with a comparatively low irritation profile. According to PubMed, a comprehensive 2025 review of azelaic acid's pharmacology found that 15–20% formulations produced clinically meaningful improvements with mild, transient local irritation, and that in melasma and other dyschromias, 20% azelaic acid cream demonstrated efficacy similar to hydroquinone with a more favourable safety profile (Petrovici et al., 2025). Mechanistically, azelaic acid works by inhibiting tyrosinase, the enzyme melanocytes use to synthesise melanin, while also having anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial effects on the acne lesions that are driving new pigmentation.

Niacinamide (vitamin B3) works differently: rather than inhibiting melanin production, it interferes with the transfer of melanin-containing granules from melanocytes to surrounding skin cells, which reduces visible pigmentation without directly suppressing pigment synthesis. It is also well tolerated as a daily-use ingredient and is frequently layered with azelaic acid or retinoids as a maintenance step.

Topical retinoids (tretinoin, adapalene, or the newer retinoic-acid-receptor-selective trifarotene) accelerate skin cell turnover, which physically speeds the clearance of epidermal pigment while also treating the acne itself. According to PubMed, a real-world clinical experience report from the Italian Acne Board notes increasing evidence that retinoids can improve both acne-induced scarring and acne-induced macular hyperpigmentation, and that trifarotene in particular plays an important role in patients predisposed to hyperpigmentation as part of a long-term maintenance strategy (Annunziata et al., 2025). Retinoids are typically introduced gradually (starting a few nights a week) since irritation itself can, ironically, trigger more PIH in reactive skin types if introduced too aggressively.

Step 3: Hydroquinone — More Potent, Needs Supervision

Hydroquinone remains one of the most effective depigmenting agents available, working by inhibiting tyrosinase more potently than azelaic acid. According to PubMed, hydroquinone is listed among the standard topical options for PIH in current review literature, typically used in short, supervised courses rather than indefinitely (Yadav et al., 2026). Because prolonged, unsupervised use carries a risk of irritation, rebound pigmentation, or (rarely, with long-term high-concentration use) exogenous ochronosis — a separate, difficult-to-treat pigmentation complication — dermatologist oversight, defined treatment cycles, and often a cycled or combination-formula approach are standard practice rather than open-ended self-treatment.

Step 4: Procedural Options — Chemical Peels and Conservative-Setting Lasers

When topicals plateau, or when PIH is more extensive, procedural options can accelerate results — but the settings matter more in darker skin than in any other patient group, because aggressive heat or exfoliation can itself trigger new PIH rather than clear the old.

Superficial chemical peels — typically glycolic acid or salicylic acid at conservative concentrations — work by controlled exfoliation of the epidermis, which speeds the shedding of pigmented skin cells while also treating comedonal and inflammatory acne. Our chemical peel service uses this same principle: superficial, repeated peels rather than a single aggressive one, which is the safer approach for Fitzpatrick IV–VI skin. According to PubMed, published PIH management reviews list glycolic acid and salicylic acid chemical peels alongside topical agents and light/laser-based therapies as standard treatment options, while also noting that high-level clinical evidence for comparative efficacy between these modalities remains limited (Yadav et al., 2026) — which is why peel strength and interval are adjusted conservatively and escalated only as tolerance is confirmed.

Q-switched and picosecond lasers deliver very short pulses of energy that fragment excess melanin without significant heat diffusion into surrounding tissue — the same principle used for pigmented lesions and tattoo removal, adapted to conservative settings for diffuse PIH. According to PubMed, a 2026 prospective case series specifically evaluated a 755 nm picosecond laser (PicoSure) in patients with Fitzpatrick skin types III–V for pigmentary conditions, reporting significant pigmentation improvement with minimal PIH observed as a side effect and high patient satisfaction (Sethi et al., 2026) — supporting the idea that shorter-pulse, lower-fluence laser settings are the safer entry point in darker skin, where standard higher-fluence protocols carry a real risk of triggering the same pigmentation problem they are meant to treat. Our Q-switch vs. chemical peel comparison covers how we choose between these two procedural routes for a given patient's pigmentation pattern. For patients whose PIH sits on an acne-prone, oilier skin type, a carbon laser facial is sometimes used as a complementary step, since it combines gentle exfoliation with oil-gland calming that can reduce the frequency of new acne lesions feeding the pigmentation cycle.

A related, common source of confusion is what to expect from the skin in the days immediately after starting a new topical or peel protocol — some initial flare-up of surface texture is a normal adjustment phase and shouldn't be mistaken for a reaction to stop treatment. We cover how to tell the difference between purging and a genuine breakout in a separate guide.

How the Ladder Is Usually Sequenced in Practice

In practice, the steps above are not strictly linear — they are typically layered. A common approach starts with strict sun protection and active acne control from day one, adds azelaic acid and/or niacinamide as the foundational daily topicals, introduces a retinoid once the skin tolerates it, and reserves hydroquinone for more stubborn or extensive PIH under supervision. Procedural options — superficial peels first, conservative-setting laser second — are generally added once topical therapy has been given adequate time (often 8–12 weeks) to show its full effect, since PIH resolution is inherently gradual and premature escalation to procedures doesn't necessarily speed things up if the underlying acne and photoprotection aren't already controlled.

Dr. Punit Sakhare notes that in clinical practice, the patients who see the most consistent improvement are the ones who stay consistent with sunscreen and their topical regimen between procedural sessions, rather than treating a single peel or laser session as a stand-alone fix.

The Bottom Line

PIH after acne is common, especially in Fitzpatrick IV–VI skin, and it is treatable — but it responds to a structured, patient sequence rather than any single product or single procedure. The published evidence supports starting with photoprotection and well-tolerated topicals like azelaic acid, niacinamide, and retinoids, reserving hydroquinone for supervised use, and adding procedural options like superficial chemical peels or conservative-setting lasers once topical therapy has had time to work. Because darker skin is more reactive to inflammation and to aggressive procedural settings alike, the safest and most effective path is usually the more conservative, better-sequenced one — not the most intensive one.
