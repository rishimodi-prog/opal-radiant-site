Laser Hair Removal and Pregnancy: Is It Safe? A Review of the Safety Data

By Dr. Punit Sakhare, MD Dermatologist · 2026-07-28 · 9 min read

Quick Facts

The short answer
Most dermatologists recommend deferring elective laser hair removal during pregnancy — not because harm has been proven, but because of what remains unknown and how pregnancy itself changes the skin and hair being treated.
Why hormones matter
Rising estrogen and progesterone prolong the hair growth (anagen) phase during pregnancy, then trigger a shedding phase afterward — making treatment response unpredictable during pregnancy specifically.
Skin sensitivity
Increased melanocyte-stimulating hormone activity in pregnancy drives conditions like melasma and linea nigra, raising the theoretical risk of pigment changes from melanin-targeting lasers.
The evidence gap
Pregnant women are routinely excluded from cosmetic laser device trials, so dedicated pregnancy safety data doesn't exist — this is an absence of evidence, not evidence of danger.
When to resume
Most clinics recommend waiting until postpartum, once hormone levels and hair-growth cycling have stabilized, and after breastfeeding has ended if applicable.

This question comes up often at consultation, and it deserves a straight answer: most dermatologist-led clinics, including ours, recommend waiting until after pregnancy to start or continue laser hair removal. That recommendation is easy to misread as "laser hair removal is dangerous during pregnancy" — but that isn't quite what the evidence says, and being evidence-honest means being precise about the difference.

This article covers three things: what the laser itself physically does (and why that hasn't changed since our earlier review of the physics), how pregnancy hormones alter the hair and skin being treated, and — the part that's easy to gloss over — why dedicated safety trials in pregnant populations essentially don't exist, and what that absence actually means.

What the Laser Itself Does: A Quick Recap

Laser hair removal works through selective photothermolysis: a wavelength of light (typically 755–1064 nm, depending on the device) is preferentially absorbed by melanin in the hair follicle and converted to localized heat, disabling the follicle while sparing surrounding tissue. As covered in more depth in our piece on whether laser hair removal causes cancer, this is a non-ionizing, thermal effect confined to the outer few millimetres of skin — it does not involve ionizing radiation, and it does not have a known mechanism for reaching the bloodstream or a developing fetus.

On pure physics grounds, there is no obvious reason to expect the laser itself to pose a direct risk to a pregnancy. That reassurance is real, but it's only one part of the picture — and it's the part that hasn't actually changed for a pregnant patient. What has changed is the body underneath the laser: the hair-growth cycle the laser is trying to target, and the pigment-cell behaviour of the skin the laser is passing through. Both are temporarily altered by pregnancy hormones, and both are relevant to how predictably a laser-tissue interaction plays out — independent of any question about the laser's intrinsic safety.

So if the mechanism hasn't changed, why the caution? Three separate reasons, each independent of the other, add up to the standard precautionary advice: pregnancy alters the hair-growth cycle being targeted, it alters the skin's pigment behaviour in ways relevant to a melanin-seeking laser, and — the reason that's most often left unsaid — there is essentially no dedicated clinical trial data on laser hair removal specifically in pregnant women to fall back on. None of these are the same as "proven harm." All three are legitimate reasons to be cautious anyway.

Reason One: Pregnancy Changes the Hair You're Treating

Hair grows in cycles — an active growth phase (anagen), a brief transitional phase (catagen), and a resting phase (telogen) before the hair sheds. Laser hair removal is most effective on hairs actively in anagen, since that's when the follicle has the melanin-rich structure the laser targets; hairs currently in telogen are largely untouched by a given session, which is exactly why any laser hair removal course, pregnant or not, normally needs several sessions spaced weeks apart to catch hairs as they cycle into anagen.

Pregnancy disrupts this cycle further. Elevated estrogen and progesterone are understood to prolong the anagen phase across the body, which is part of why many pregnant women notice thicker, fuller-seeming hair growth, sometimes including areas not normally hair-bearing — a pattern reviewed as one of several physiological, hormone-driven skin and hair changes of pregnancy (Wilson, Pendergraft & Daniel, 2025). A comparable pattern of pregnancy-related hirsutism and altered hair physiology is also documented in a broader review of physiological skin changes in pregnancy (Tyler, 2015).

After delivery, hormone levels fall sharply and a large proportion of those follicles shift into telogen simultaneously, producing the noticeable postpartum shedding many new mothers experience a few months after birth — a hair-cycle disturbance first systematically classified as telogen effluvium (Headington, 1993). The practical implication for treatment is straightforward: a hair-growth cycle that is temporarily lengthened by hormones mid-pregnancy, and then reset again postpartum, is a moving target. Sessions scheduled during this window are working against a cycle that behaves differently than it will before or after, which can make the number of sessions needed and the results achieved difficult to predict reliably — a practical problem for treatment planning that exists independent of any safety question.

Reason Two: Pregnant Skin Reacts Differently to Melanin-Targeting Light

Because laser hair removal works by targeting melanin, how a person's skin handles melanin matters. Pregnancy is well documented to increase melanocyte activity — the cells that produce pigment — largely driven by rising estrogen, progesterone, and melanocyte-stimulating hormone. This is why conditions like melasma (patchy facial pigmentation), linea nigra, and darkening of the areola are common and considered normal, physiologic findings in pregnancy rather than disease (Tyler, 2015; Bieber, Martires, Stein, et al., 2017).

This doesn't mean laser hair removal causes these changes, and it doesn't reintroduce any cancer concern — as covered in our separate review of that question, the laser's thermal effect is unrelated to the mechanisms that cause malignancy. What it does mean is that skin with heightened, hormonally-driven pigment activity carries a less predictable risk profile for the ordinary, already-known cosmetic side effects of laser treatment — transient post-inflammatory pigment changes or uneven response — since the laser-tissue interaction depends heavily on how much melanin is present and where. Treating skin whose pigment behaviour is actively in flux makes those outcomes harder to anticipate than they are outside pregnancy.

Reason Three: There Isn't Dedicated Pregnancy Safety Trial Data — And That's Worth Saying Plainly

This is the most important reason, and the easiest one for a clinic to underplay. A systematic review specifically examining elective laser therapy during pregnancy across dermatology, urology, obstetrics, and surgery found only a limited body of literature overall, and concluded that the use of lasers in pregnant patients "is still controversial" for several applications, with hair removal captured within that broader, thinly-studied category (Allameh, Javadi, Dadkhahfar, et al., 2021).

The reason for this gap is not unique to lasers. Pregnant women are, as a matter of standard research ethics, almost universally excluded from elective and cosmetic device trials — the same pattern seen across most cosmetic dermatology research, not a red flag specific to laser hair removal. That means the large safety studies clinics normally cite for laser hair removal in the general population were not designed to include, and generally did not include, pregnant participants. An absence of dedicated trial data is not the same as evidence of harm — but it does mean clinics genuinely cannot point to trial-level reassurance for pregnant patients the way they can for the general population, and saying so plainly is more honest than implying a certainty the data doesn't support either way. This is exactly the gap a genuinely evidence-based answer has to name directly rather than talk around.

This Fits a Broader Pattern in Dermatologic Guidance During Pregnancy

Laser hair removal isn't treated as an unusual case — it fits the same general pattern dermatology follows for most elective, non-urgent cosmetic procedures during pregnancy. Reviews of dermatologic conditions in pregnancy consistently draw a distinction between skin issues that need active management during pregnancy because they affect maternal or fetal wellbeing, and the far larger category of benign, hormonally-driven changes that are best explained, reassured, and left to resolve on their own timeline (Wilson, Pendergraft & Daniel, 2025; Tyler, 2015). Elective cosmetic treatments — laser hair removal included — sit outside the category that requires action during pregnancy, which is a large part of why deferring them carries little downside.

This is also why the precautionary approach isn't really about laser hair removal being unusually risky compared with other cosmetic procedures. It reflects a broader, sensible default in dermatology: when a procedure is optional and its timing is flexible, and the patient population undergoing it has temporarily changed physiology plus no dedicated safety data, the lowest-friction choice is to wait rather than to treat absence of evidence as license to proceed.

What a Precautionary Approach Looks Like in Practice

Given all three factors together — no established direct-harm mechanism, but genuinely altered hair and skin physiology and no dedicated trial reassurance — the standard, dermatologist-led approach is to treat laser hair removal as an elective procedure that can safely wait. Dr. Punit Sakhare's guidance to patients follows this same reasoning: pause active treatment plans on learning of a pregnancy, and resume once hormone levels and hair-growth cycling have settled postpartum, factoring in a period after breastfeeding ends as well, since prolactin can also influence hair cycling during lactation.

For patients returning to treatment after delivery, it's worth expecting that regrowth patterns may look different from before pregnancy, given the hair-cycle disruption described above — a topic we cover in detail separately: what to expect from laser hair removal regrowth after pregnancy and hormonal change. Our laser hair removal service page has details on resuming a treatment plan and what a postpartum consultation involves.

The Bottom Line

There is no known biological mechanism by which the non-ionizing wavelengths used in laser hair removal would directly harm a pregnancy. At the same time, there is no dedicated clinical trial evidence confirming its safety in pregnant populations either, because pregnant women are routinely excluded from this research — and pregnancy independently alters the hair and skin being treated in ways that make outcomes less predictable regardless of the safety question. Taken together, that's why the responsible, evidence-based recommendation from dermatology-led clinics is to defer elective laser hair removal during pregnancy and resume postpartum, rather than to claim it is either proven dangerous or proven risk-free.
