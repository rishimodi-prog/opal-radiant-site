Fitzpatrick Skin Typing: Why Indian Skin Needs Different Laser Settings (and How to Check Yours)

By Dr. Punit Sakhare, MD Dermatologist · 2026-07-22 · 9 min read

Quick Facts

What it measures
The Fitzpatrick scale (I-VI) classifies skin by how it reacts to UV exposure — burning versus tanning tendency — not simply visible skin color.
Where Indian skin typically falls
Most Indian skin is classified Fitzpatrick III-V, with some individuals at VI, according to dermatology literature on Asian and South Asian skin.
Why it matters for lasers
Higher Fitzpatrick types carry more epidermal melanin, which competes with the hair follicle for laser energy — raising burn and pigment-change risk if wavelength and fluence aren't adjusted.
Safer wavelength for darker skin
Longer wavelengths (Nd:YAG, 1064 nm) penetrate deeper and are absorbed less by epidermal melanin, making them the preferred choice for Fitzpatrick IV-VI.
How it's assessed
Self-assessment (sun/tan history) gives an approximate type; clinics refine this with visual exam and tools like a mexameter before setting laser parameters.

Every laser hair removal consultation should start with a question that has nothing to do with hair: what is your skin's Fitzpatrick type? This isn't a formality. The Fitzpatrick scale, a six-category classification of how skin responds to ultraviolet light, is the single biggest variable in deciding which laser wavelength, fluence, and pulse duration are safe to use — and getting it wrong is the most common cause of burns and pigment changes in laser treatments on Indian skin.

This guide explains what the Fitzpatrick scale actually measures, where Indian skin typically sits on it, the melanin physics behind why it changes laser settings, and how you can estimate your own approximate type before your next consultation.

What the Fitzpatrick Scale Actually Measures

The Fitzpatrick scale was developed by Harvard dermatologist Thomas B. Fitzpatrick and formally validated in a 1988 paper in the Archives of Dermatology, originally to standardize UV dosing for psoralen-UVA (PUVA) phototherapy in psoriasis patients (Fitzpatrick, 1988). It was later adopted across dermatology and cosmetic medicine as a general framework for predicting how skin will respond to sunlight and, by extension, to energy-based devices like lasers.

The scale has six types, based on self-reported burning and tanning behavior after roughly 30-45 minutes of unprotected midday sun exposure, not on visually judged skin color alone:

  • Type I — always burns, never tans (very fair skin, often red or blonde hair)
  • Type II — usually burns, tans minimally
  • Type III — sometimes has mild burning, tans uniformly
  • Type IV — burns minimally, always tans well (moderate brown)
  • Type V — rarely burns, tans profusely (dark brown)
  • Type VI — never burns, deeply pigmented (black)

The key point often missed: this was designed as a burn/tan-response scale, not a skin-color chart. Two people with similar visible skin tone can have different Fitzpatrick types depending on how their skin actually reacts to UV, which is why self-reported sun history is still part of a proper assessment.

Where Indian Skin Typically Falls: Mostly III-V, Sometimes Higher

Indian skin is genetically diverse across regions, but the dermatology literature is fairly consistent on the general range. A review of Asian skin characteristics in the International Journal of Dermatology notes that Asian populations span a range of phototypes, from Fitzpatrick III-IV in Chinese and Japanese skin to "type IV and V in Indian and Pakistani people" (Chan et al., 2018). Some individuals, particularly from southern India or with more sun-exposed lifestyles, fall into type VI.

The same review explains part of the mechanism: the number of melanocytes (the pigment-producing cells) is roughly similar across all skin types, but higher-phototype skin has melanocytes that produce more melanin, distributed as smaller, more numerous melanosomes spread more broadly through the keratinocyte layers of the epidermis (Chan et al., 2018). This gives darker skin types meaningfully more natural UV photoprotection — but it's the same property that changes how a laser interacts with the skin.

Because type isn't fixed — it shifts somewhat with tanning, seasonal sun exposure, and geography — assuming a type based on ethnicity alone is a mistake. "I'm Indian, so I'm probably a IV" is a reasonable starting guess, not a substitute for individual assessment.

The Melanin Physics: Why Skin Type Changes How a Laser Behaves

Laser hair removal works through selective photothermolysis: the laser wavelength is chosen because it's preferentially absorbed by melanin, the pigment concentrated in the hair follicle. That absorbed light converts to heat, disabling the follicle, while surrounding tissue with less melanin stays relatively untouched.

The problem in higher Fitzpatrick types is that the epidermis itself now contains substantially more melanin as background pigment. That epidermal melanin becomes a competing chromophore — it absorbs and scatters some of the laser energy that was meant to travel down to the follicle, converting to heat within the epidermis itself rather than at the target. This is the direct physical reason darker skin carries higher risk of epidermal burns, blistering, and transient hyperpigmentation or hypopigmentation when laser parameters aren't adjusted for skin type.

This isn't theoretical. A frequently cited review of adverse effects across large laser hair removal safety studies found that pigment-change rates varied sharply by device and skin type — up to roughly 19% with some shorter-wavelength devices, compared with 2-3% using the longer-wavelength Nd:YAG laser more commonly matched to darker skin (Lim & Lanigan, 2006). The rate difference isn't about the laser being inherently "riskier" — it's about wavelength selection matching (or not matching) the patient's epidermal melanin load.

How This Translates to Laser Selection and Settings

In practice, this melanin physics is exactly why clinics don't use one laser setting for every patient. A clinical guidelines review in the American Journal of Clinical Dermatology lays out the tradeoffs across the main device types — ruby (694 nm), alexandrite (755 nm), diode (~800 nm), and Nd:YAG (1064 nm) (Liew, 2002):

  • Shorter wavelengths (ruby, alexandrite) have higher affinity for melanin, making them efficient hair-reduction tools in lighter skin (roughly Fitzpatrick I-III), but riskier in darker skin because more energy is absorbed at the epidermal surface.
  • Nd:YAG (1064 nm) penetrates deeper into the skin and is absorbed less by epidermal melanin at the surface, which is why it's generally considered more suited to darker skin types (IV-VI) — the same review notes it can be somewhat less efficacious for hair reduction than shorter wavelengths, a tradeoff clinics accept deliberately for safety in darker skin (Liew, 2002).
  • Diode lasers sit in between, and are commonly used on Indian skin with adjusted fluence (energy density), longer pulse duration, and epidermal cooling to manage the same risk.

The same review notes that patients with Fitzpatrick IV and V skin can be treated effectively with hair-reduction outcomes comparable to lighter skin types, provided fluence and pulse duration are tailored appropriately and cooling is used during treatment (Liew, 2002). The point isn't that darker skin can't be treated safely — it's that "safely" requires deliberately different settings, not the same protocol used on lighter skin.

Self-Assessing Your Approximate Fitzpatrick Type

You can get a reasonable approximation of your own Fitzpatrick type by honestly answering a few questions about how your skin actually behaves in sun, not just how it looks indoors:

  • Does your skin ever burn (turn red, tender, or peel) after 30-45 minutes of unprotected midday sun, or does it go straight to tanning?
  • If it does burn, how often — rarely, sometimes, or almost always?
  • How deep is your natural tan at its darkest, and how long does it take to fade?
  • What is your natural eye and hair color (a secondary indicator, weighted less heavily than sun response)?

Most Indian readers who tan easily, rarely or never burn, and have naturally medium-to-deep brown skin will self-assess somewhere in the III-V range, consistent with the population data above. But this is an approximation, not a diagnosis. In clinic, Dr. Sakhare's assessment adds a visual exam and, where relevant, objective tools such as a mexameter or colorimeter to measure melanin content directly rather than relying on self-report alone — the same category of device used in published Indian pigmentation studies to quantify skin tone changes (Gabhane et al., 2025). Photoprotection matters here too: because higher Fitzpatrick skin is more prone to post-procedure pigment shifts, consistent daily sunscreen use before and after any energy-based treatment reduces that risk — a topic covered in more depth in our SPF guide for Indian skin.

It's Not Just Lasers: Fitzpatrick Typing Affects Other Treatments Too

Skin typing isn't unique to hair removal. Chemical peels carry a similar logic: higher-Fitzpatrick skin has a greater tendency toward post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) after inflammation of any kind, including a peel that's too strong or too aggressive for the skin's tolerance. This is why peel strength, depth, and downtime protocols are chosen based on the same type assessment used for lasers, not a one-size-fits-all concentration. Any energy-based or chemically active treatment on skin — laser, peel, or otherwise — benefits from the same starting question: what is this skin's actual Fitzpatrick type, and how does that change the plan?

The Bottom Line

The Fitzpatrick scale isn't a formality on an intake form — it's the variable that determines which laser wavelength is safe, how much energy can be used, and what side-effect risk to expect. Most Indian skin sits in the III-V range, with more epidermal melanin than lighter phototypes, which is exactly why laser hair removal in Mumbai clinics that treat Indian skin regularly lean toward longer-wavelength devices and more conservative settings by default, rather than importing protocols designed for lighter-skinned populations. If you've had a laser session that left more redness, burning, or pigment change than expected, mismatched wavelength or settings for your actual skin type is one of the first things worth asking about — see our related review of laser hair removal side effects by skin type for a fuller breakdown, and our piece on whether laser hair removal causes cancer if radiation safety, rather than pigment risk, is your main concern.
