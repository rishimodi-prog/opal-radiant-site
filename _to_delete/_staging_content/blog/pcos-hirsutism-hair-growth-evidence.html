PCOS and Excess Hair Growth (Hirsutism): What the Endocrinology Evidence Says About Long-Term Management

By Dr. Punit Sakhare, MD Dermatologist · 2026-08-01 · 9 min read

Quick Facts

The root cause
Hirsutism in PCOS is driven by hyperandrogenism — excess androgens acting on hair follicles — not by a problem with the hair or skin itself.
How common
PCOS is the most common identifiable cause of hirsutism; roughly 70-80% of women with confirmed androgen excess show it, though prevalence and visibility vary by ethnicity (Azziz, 2003).
The insulin link
Insulin resistance is present in the majority of women with PCOS and works with hyperandrogenism in a self-reinforcing "vicious cycle" that raises free testosterone (Szkodziak et al., 2025).
How it's measured
The modified Ferriman-Gallwey score visually rates terminal hair growth across 9 body areas on a 0-4 scale; a total score above the population-specific threshold defines clinical hirsutism.
Where laser fits
Laser hair removal disables existing terminal hair follicles but does not change circulating androgen levels — published reviews describe it as an adjunct within combination therapy, not a stand-alone cure.
Why it responds slowly
Hirsutism improves more slowly than acne with hormonal therapy — typically 3-8 months — because already-terminalized hairs must complete their growth cycle before medical treatment shows visible effect (Lizneva et al., 2016).

If you have PCOS (polycystic ovary syndrome) and deal with excess dark, coarse hair on the face, chest, or abdomen, you've probably already tried removing it. Our earlier post on laser hair removal for PCOS covers the practical side of that — what sessions look like, how many you'll likely need, and how to prepare. This article goes a level deeper: why PCOS causes this pattern of hair growth in the first place, what the endocrinology literature says about the hormonal and metabolic mechanisms behind it, and what the evidence actually shows about combining a physical treatment like laser with medical management of the underlying hormones. It's the companion piece for readers who want to understand the biology, not just the treatment steps — and if scalp thinning rather than facial or body hair is your main concern, our related piece on PCOS and hair loss covers the same hormone family acting in the opposite direction.

The Hormonal Root Cause: Hyperandrogenism and the Hair Follicle

Hirsutism is formally defined as excess terminal (coarse, pigmented) hair growth in women in a pattern typically seen in men — face, chest, upper abdomen, and inner thighs. According to a review on androgen excess published in Best Practice & Research Clinical Obstetrics & Gynaecology, this is a direct downstream sign of hyperandrogenism: elevated testosterone and related androgens, produced predominantly by the ovaries (and to a lesser extent the adrenal glands), acting on hair follicles that are genetically primed to respond to androgen stimulation (Lizneva et al., 2016).

Not every hair follicle responds to androgens the same way. Follicles on the face, chest, and lower abdomen are androgen-sensitive in a stimulatory direction — exposure converts fine, light vellus hair into thicker, darker terminal hair. This is the opposite of what happens on the scalp, where androgen-sensitive follicles in genetically predisposed women miniaturize instead, which is why hirsutism and androgen-driven scalp thinning can occur in the same patient from the same hormonal cause.

A classic review in Obstetrics & Gynecology notes that PCOS is by far the most common underlying cause of hirsutism, though a minority of hirsute women have normal androgen levels ("idiopathic hirsutism") or a different endocrine cause entirely, such as nonclassic congenital adrenal hyperplasia — which is why a proper diagnostic work-up, not just visual assessment, matters before starting treatment (Azziz, 2003).

The Insulin Resistance Connection: A Self-Reinforcing Cycle

PCOS hirsutism is rarely just a hormone-in-isolation problem. Insulin resistance — reduced sensitivity of body tissue to insulin, requiring the pancreas to produce more of it — is present in a majority of women with PCOS and interacts directly with androgen production. A 2025 study in Scientific Reports analyzing 200 women with PCOS across four clinical phenotypes found insulin resistance (measured by HOMA-IR, a standard lab-based index) in 57.5% of participants overall, with levels elevated above reference thresholds in every phenotype studied (Szkodziak et al., 2025).

The same study describes what researchers call a "vicious cycle": elevated insulin stimulates the ovaries to produce more androgens directly, and separately reduces the liver's production of sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG) — a protein that normally binds testosterone and keeps it biologically inactive. Less SHBG means more free (active) testosterone circulating and reaching hair follicles. The Szkodziak study found a positive correlation between the Free Androgen Index, HOMA-IR, and Ferriman-Gallwey hirsutism scores, supporting this mechanistic link rather than treating androgens and insulin resistance as separate, unrelated problems.

This is clinically relevant beyond hair: it's the reason insulin-sensitizing approaches — lifestyle changes, weight management where indicated, and medications like metformin — appear in PCOS hirsutism management guidance rather than being treated as unrelated to a cosmetic complaint.

How Hirsutism Is Actually Measured: The Ferriman-Gallwey Score

Because "excess hair" is subjective, clinicians use a standardized visual tool called the modified Ferriman-Gallwey (mFG) score to make hirsutism assessment consistent and comparable across research and practice. The method rates terminal hair growth across nine androgen-sensitive body areas — including the upper lip, chin, chest, upper and lower abdomen, and thighs — on a 0 (no terminal hair) to 4 (extensive terminal hair) scale, producing a total composite score (Lizneva et al., 2016).

A higher score indicates more extensive androgen-driven hair growth, and the score is one part — not the whole — of a hyperandrogenism work-up. The same review notes that biochemical assessment (blood testosterone, and in less clear-cut cases DHEAS or androstenedione) is particularly important when hirsutism is mild or absent despite other PCOS features, since the mFG score alone can under-detect hyperandrogenism in some populations and doesn't determine which specific treatment pathway a patient needs (Lizneva et al., 2016).

This is also why threshold scores differ by population and why a single self-assessed "is this a lot of hair" judgment isn't a diagnosis — it's a starting point for a clinical conversation, ideally alongside a dermatologist or endocrinologist who can interpret it against bloodwork and menstrual history.

Why Laser Hair Removal Alone Doesn't Address the Root Cause

Laser hair removal works through selective photothermolysis: light energy absorbed by melanin in the hair shaft and follicle converts to heat, damaging the follicle's ability to produce new hair. This is a physical, local effect on hair that has already become terminal — it does nothing to circulating androgen levels, insulin sensitivity, or the follicle's underlying receptor sensitivity to androgens elsewhere on the body.

A review on treating hirsutism and acne in hyperandrogenism, published in Best Practice & Research Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism, is direct about this limitation: laser photothermolysis and electrolysis are considered the most effective cosmetic procedures for existing hair, but "the effects of these methods should not be considered permanent," and drugs targeting androgen secretion or action remain necessary to address hair growth at its source (Moghetti & Toscano, 2006).

In practice, this means that as long as the underlying hyperandrogenism is untreated, hormone-driven follicles that haven't yet terminalized can still be recruited into producing new coarse hair over time — even after a course of laser has successfully cleared existing hair. This is precisely why the literature repeatedly frames laser as one component of a broader plan rather than an endpoint in itself.

What the Evidence Says About Combining Laser With Medical Management

Multiple independent reviews converge on the same structural recommendation: combination therapy outperforms any single approach for PCOS-driven hirsutism. The Obstetrics & Gynecology review by Azziz frames evidence-based management as three parallel tracks used together — hormonal suppression (combined oral contraceptives, GnRH analogues, or insulin sensitizers), peripheral androgen blockade (anti-androgens such as spironolactone, cyproterone acetate, flutamide, or 5-alpha-reductase inhibitors like finasteride), and mechanical or cosmetic hair removal, explicitly including laser hair removal and electrolysis (Azziz, 2003).

The Lizneva review reinforces the same sequencing logic and adds an important timeline detail: acne generally responds to hormonal therapy within weeks, but hirsutism is markedly slower, with initial improvement typically visible only around 3 months and fuller response often taking 6-8 months of consistent hormonal therapy (Lizneva et al., 2016). This mismatch in timelines is one practical reason clinics often start laser hair removal for the visible, distressing hair immediately, while hormonal or insulin-sensitizing therapy — prescribed and monitored by a physician — works in parallel on the underlying driver over a longer horizon.

Where insulin resistance is confirmed or strongly suspected, metformin and related insulin-sensitizing approaches are commonly integrated into this plan; a broader PCOS management review notes that insulin sensitizers, alongside lifestyle and dietary intervention, are core strategies for addressing the metabolic drivers that feed into hyperandrogenism (Diamanti & La Marca, 2025) — reinforcing why a hirsutism plan built around laser alone, without any hormonal or metabolic assessment, is treating a symptom without addressing why it recurs.

Building a Long-Term, Evidence-Based Plan

Pulling the literature together, a realistic evidence-based sequence for PCOS-related hirsutism looks like this: confirm the diagnosis and hirsutism severity with a clinician using bloodwork and an mFG assessment; start laser hair removal for existing terminal hair, since it produces the fastest visible improvement and doesn't need to wait on hormonal response; and in parallel, have a physician evaluate whether hormonal therapy (oral contraceptives or anti-androgens), an insulin sensitizer, or both are appropriate based on your specific hormone and metabolic profile.

Expect the hormonal side to take months rather than weeks to show effect, and understand that laser results depend on the hair being in an active growth phase at each session — which is standard practice across our laser hair removal service and applies the same way regardless of the underlying cause of hair growth. Because hyperandrogenism is a chronic hormonal condition rather than a one-time event, most patients need an ongoing plan — periodic laser maintenance alongside continued medical management — rather than a fixed course that ends once hair is initially cleared.
