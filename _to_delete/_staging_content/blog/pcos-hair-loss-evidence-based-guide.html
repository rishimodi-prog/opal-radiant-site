PCOS and Hair Loss: The Evidence-Based Guide to Androgenic Alopecia in Women

By Dr. Punit Sakhare, MD Dermatologist · 2026-07-19 · 9 min read

Quick Facts

What it's called clinically
Female pattern hair loss (FPHL) with androgen excess — not the same condition as sudden diffuse shedding (telogen effluvium).
Root mechanism
Elevated androgens are converted to DHT by the enzyme 5-alpha reductase in genetically sensitive scalp follicles, shortening the hair growth phase over time.
Where it shows up
Diffuse thinning at the crown and central scalp, often with a widening centre parting — the frontal hairline is usually preserved, unlike male-pattern baldness.
How common in PCOS
Alopecia is one of the recognised dermatologic signs of PCOS, alongside acne, hirsutism, and acanthosis nigricans, though it is a less consistent marker of hyperandrogenism than hirsutism.
First-line evidence-based treatment
Topical minoxidil 5%, with anti-androgens (e.g. spironolactone) added for more severe or hormonally-driven cases, per Androgen Excess and PCOS Society guidance.
Adjunct in-clinic option
Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) shows a positive effect on hair density in meta-analyses of female pattern hair loss, typically used alongside — not instead of — medical therapy.

If you have PCOS (polycystic ovary syndrome) and have noticed more hair in your brush, a widening centre parting, or a scalp that shows through more than it used to, you're dealing with a different problem than excess facial or body hair. That's hirsutism — covered in detail in our companion piece on PCOS and unwanted hair growth, and also touched on in our earlier post on laser hair removal for PCOS. This article is about the opposite symptom on the same spectrum: scalp hair loss and thinning driven by the same underlying hormonal biology. The short version is that both conditions trace back to elevated androgens, but they affect hair in opposite ways depending on which follicles are involved and how those follicles respond to hormone signalling. Below is what the endocrinology and dermatology literature actually says about why this happens and what has evidence behind it as treatment.

Why PCOS Causes Hair Loss on the Scalp While Causing Hair Growth Elsewhere

This paradox confuses a lot of people, and it's worth explaining clearly because it's central to understanding your own case. PCOS is fundamentally a condition of androgen excess — elevated levels of testosterone and related hormones, produced by the ovaries and/or adrenal glands. According to a review for dermatologists published in the Journal of the American Academy of Dermatology, this hyperandrogenism is the hallmark biochemical feature of PCOS and underlies several of its cutaneous (skin-related) signs, including acne, hirsutism, and alopecia (Housman & Reynolds, 2014).

Hair follicles on different parts of the body respond to androgens in opposite ways because they carry different sensitivities and enzyme activity. Follicles on the face, chest, and lower abdomen are typically stimulated by androgens into longer, coarser growth — this is hirsutism. Scalp follicles that are genetically predisposed to androgen sensitivity respond in the reverse direction: androgen exposure progressively shortens their growth cycle, producing thinner, shorter hairs and eventual follicle miniaturisation. Both processes are driven by the same hormone family acting on genetically different tissue.

The Mechanism: DHT, 5-Alpha Reductase, and Follicle Miniaturisation

The specific molecule responsible for scalp hair thinning is dihydrotestosterone (DHT), a more potent derivative of testosterone. Testosterone is converted to DHT locally, inside the hair follicle, by an enzyme called 5-alpha reductase. In scalp follicles that carry a genetic sensitivity to androgens, DHT binds androgen receptors in the follicle's dermal papilla (the structure at the follicle base that regulates hair growth) and progressively shortens the anagen (active growth) phase of the hair cycle.

Over repeated cycles, this produces follicular miniaturisation: each regrowth cycle produces a visibly finer, shorter hair than the one before, until some follicles stop producing visible hair altogether. This is a gradual, cumulative process — it is not the same mechanism as telogen effluvium (a sudden, diffuse shed usually triggered by illness, stress, or nutrient deficiency, and often temporary). An expert task force convened by the Androgen Excess and PCOS Society formally recommends the clinical term "female pattern hair loss" (FPHL) for this androgen-driven pattern, replacing older, less precise labels (Carmina et al., 2019).

The degree of scalp-follicle sensitivity to DHT is largely inherited, which is why FPHL can run in families and why two women with similar blood androgen levels can have noticeably different degrees of visible thinning. This also explains why PCOS itself doesn't automatically cause hair loss in every patient who has it — it raises the amount of circulating androgen available for conversion to DHT, but the follicle's genetic response to that DHT determines whether visible thinning actually results.

What It Looks Like — and Why a Dermatologist's Exam Still Matters

FPHL typically presents as diffuse thinning concentrated over the crown and central scalp, sometimes described as a "Christmas tree" pattern because the parting appears wider near the front of the scalp and narrows toward the back. Unlike male-pattern baldness, the frontal hairline is generally preserved (Carmina et al., 2019).

One important nuance from the research: androgenic alopecia is a less reliable marker of hyperandrogenism than hirsutism or acne. A retrospective chart review of women presenting with hyperandrogenism found that while acanthosis nigricans and hirsutism correlated well with elevated androgen levels on lab testing, androgenic alopecia did not correlate as consistently (Clark et al., 2014). In plain terms: you can have PCOS-driven hair thinning without dramatically abnormal blood androgen levels, and conversely, hair thinning alone isn't a reliable way to diagnose PCOS. This is exactly why the Androgen Excess and PCOS Society guidance recommends that any patient presenting with FPHL undergo assessment for possible androgen excess, alongside ruling out thyroid dysfunction, iron deficiency, and other non-hormonal contributors, rather than assuming a diagnosis from hair pattern alone (Carmina et al., 2019).

Minoxidil: The Evidence-Based First-Line Treatment

Topical minoxidil is the best-studied treatment for female pattern hair loss and is recommended as first-line therapy by the Androgen Excess and PCOS Society task force, at a 5% concentration (Carmina et al., 2019). Minoxidil's mechanism is not primarily anti-androgenic — it's a vasodilator that appears to prolong the anagen (growth) phase and increase blood flow to the follicle, which is why it works across multiple causes of hair thinning, not just androgen-driven ones.

Consistency matters more than most people expect: because minoxidil works by influencing the hair cycle, visible results typically take several months of continuous daily use, and shedding can temporarily increase in the first weeks as the follicle cycle resets — this is an expected transitional effect, not a sign of worsening.

Anti-Androgens: Targeting the Hormone Pathway Directly

For more severe FPHL, or when blood tests confirm elevated androgens, guidelines support adding a 5-alpha reductase inhibitor or an anti-androgen medication to minoxidil rather than using either alone (Carmina et al., 2019). Spironolactone is the most extensively used anti-androgen for this purpose. Originally developed as a blood-pressure medication, spironolactone was found — initially by chance in a PCOS patient being treated for hypertension — to also improve hirsutism, which led to its off-label use in hormonal hair conditions (Rathnayake & Sinclair, 2010).

Spironolactone works through two mechanisms: it reduces adrenal androgen production and competitively blocks androgen receptors in target tissue, including hair follicles. A review in Dermatologic Clinics notes that decades of off-label use in FPHL show it arrests progression of hair loss in most patients, with a meaningful subset achieving partial regrowth, and describes a favourable long-term safety profile when properly monitored (Rathnayake & Sinclair, 2010). Because these medications work on the hormonal pathway rather than the scalp directly, they require a prescribing clinician to select the right agent and dose and monitor for interactions — this isn't a self-directed treatment.

Where PRP Fits: What the Meta-Analyses Actually Show

Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) involves drawing a small sample of the patient's own blood, concentrating the platelets (which release growth factors involved in tissue repair), and injecting the concentrate into the scalp. It has become a widely offered adjunct for hair thinning, including in the Androgen Excess and PCOS Society's evidence review, which lists PRP among modalities with supporting data for FPHL (Carmina et al., 2019).

A systematic review and meta-analysis focused specifically on PRP in female androgenic alopecia — pooling 16 randomised controlled trials and 26 observational studies covering 776 female participants — found a positive effect of PRP on hair density compared with control groups (Zhou et al., 2021). The authors were candid about limitations: protocols for preparing and administering PRP vary considerably between studies (number of sessions, centrifugation method, activation technique), which makes it hard to state one "standard" expected result, and they called for larger, more standardised trials. In practice, this means PRP is reasonably supported as an adjunct to medical therapy — most useful alongside minoxidil or anti-androgens rather than as a stand-alone replacement for them. For patients looking to add scalp volume alongside medical therapy, hair filler treatments are a separate, non-hormonal option worth discussing at consultation.

What This Means Practically If You Have PCOS

Because FPHL is a slow, cumulative process, earlier intervention generally preserves more existing follicles than starting treatment after significant miniaturisation has occurred — this is a general principle of the hair-cycle biology described above, not a guarantee of a specific outcome for any individual. A reasonable evidence-based sequence, drawn from the guidance above, looks like: confirm the pattern and rule out other causes (thyroid, iron, telogen effluvium) with a clinician; assess androgen levels where indicated; start topical minoxidil; add an anti-androgen if hair loss is significant or hormonally driven; and consider PRP as an adjunct once medical therapy is underway. Hormonal hair loss and hormonal hair growth (hirsutism) frequently coexist in the same patient because they share a root cause, so it's common — and clinically sensible — to address both as part of a single PCOS-informed skin and hair plan rather than treating them as unrelated concerns.

It's also worth setting realistic expectations at the outset: because androgen-driven hair loss is a chronic, ongoing process rather than a one-off event, most evidence-based treatments — minoxidil and anti-androgens included — are aimed at slowing or stabilising further thinning and supporting existing follicles, with partial regrowth as a realistic bonus in a meaningful subset of patients, rather than promising complete reversal of long-miniaturised follicles. Stopping treatment typically allows the underlying androgen effect to resume, which is why maintenance therapy, not a fixed course, is the standard approach described in the endocrinology and dermatology literature above.
